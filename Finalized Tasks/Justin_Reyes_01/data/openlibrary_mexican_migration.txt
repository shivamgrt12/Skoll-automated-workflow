SECTION A. Michoacan and central-highlands emigration context (1940-1970)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[A1] Herrera, Guillermo A. "The Bracero Line: Michoacan Migration to California
     Labor Markets, 1942-1970." University of California Press, 2014. 342 pp.
     ISBN 978-0-520-27891-3
     OpenLibrary ID: OL27834891M
     Availability: US public library circulation; Los Angeles Public Library holds 3 copies.
     Relevance: Chapter 4 covers the exact demographic wave Abuela Carmen was
     part of (Michoacana women arriving in Southern California between 1955 and
     1965 through family reunification). Chapter 7 covers the informal parish
     organizing patterns among Michoacana women in South-Central and Gardena
     parishes 1965-1975. Cite for framing, not for individual claims.

[A2] Rosales, Fernanda M. "Bracero Aftermath: Family Reunification Migration
     from Central Mexico to Southern California, 1955-1970." Duke University
     Press, 2019. 288 pp.
     ISBN 978-1-4780-0184-2
     OpenLibrary ID: OL28912847M
     Availability: US academic library circulation; UCLA Library holds 4 copies.
     Relevance: covers the 1958 arrival pattern (bus travel via Zamora and
     Guadalajara, northbound train, Nogales crossing, receiving family members
     already in South Central Los Angeles). Chapter 3 has the exact South
     Central to Gardena migration triangle Carmen lived inside for six decades.
     Statistical framing only; do not cite for specific family narratives.

[A3] Alvarez, Rodolfo. "La Raza in the Land of the Free: Mexican-American
     Communities in Postwar Los Angeles County, 1945-1975." UCLA Chicano
     Studies Research Center, 2011. 456 pp.
     ISBN 978-0-89551-152-4
     OpenLibrary ID: OL25631204M
     Availability: US academic + community circulation; Cal State Dominguez
     Hills holds a special collection.
     Relevance: Chapter 9 covers the South Central and South Bay industrial
     labor market (canneries, garment shops, aerospace parts fabricators) that
     employed the 1958-1965 arrival cohort. Chapter 11 covers the parish
     organizing at St. Anne's downtown and St. Anthony's in Gardena briefly
     (three pages, footnote 47) but does not name individual congregants.

SECTION B. Catholic parish organizing and Spanish-language catechism programs
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[B1] Vasquez-Tudela, Elena. "Rezar en Espanol: The Fight for Spanish-Language
     Catholic Programs in the American Southwest, 1960-1985." University of
     Notre Dame Press, 2016. 312 pp.
     ISBN 978-0-268-10084-1
     OpenLibrary ID: OL27129084M
     Availability: US academic library circulation; St. Anthony's parish
     (Gardena) holds a copy in the rectory library.
     Relevance: covers the exact 1968 organizing that produced Gardena's
     first Spanish-language catechism program at St. Anthony's. Chapter 5
     names the eleven-woman committee Carmen was part of, though it names
     only three of them (Elvira Contreras, Rosalba Munoz, and Carmen
     "Reyes-Michoacan" - Carmen). Verify parish records before citing
     individual names.

[B2] O'Malley, Sean P. "The Second Vatican Council and the American Latino
     Parish: Local Reception, 1965-1980." Georgetown University Press, 2018.
     398 pp.
     ISBN 978-1-62616-543-8
     OpenLibrary ID: OL28001472M
     Availability: US academic library circulation.
     Relevance: framing chapter on how Vatican II created the vernacular
     opening the eleven Gardena women organized inside. Cite for the
     theological and institutional shift only; the local organizing was
     theirs, not Rome's.

SECTION C. Labor and cannery work in South Central Los Angeles 1955-1975
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[C1] Kim, Sujin. "Cannery Row Reversed: Latina Fruit-Processing Labor in
     South Central Los Angeles, 1950-1975." Stanford University Press, 2021.
     278 pp.
     ISBN 978-1-5036-1892-4
     OpenLibrary ID: OL29847213M
     Availability: US academic and community library circulation.
     Relevance: chapter 6 covers the exact Alameda Street cannery corridor
     where Carmen worked from 1958 to 1962 (apricots, peaches, pears
     through the season). Chapter 8 covers the informal English-teaching
     circles that formed on lunch breaks. This is the single closest match
     in the bibliography to Carmen's own labor history. Cite carefully;
     the book names generalized patterns, not individuals.

[C2] Diaz, Maria Elena. "Cocinar es Recordar: Kitchen Labor and Cultural
     Memory in Mexican-American Households, 1950-1990." University of Texas
     Press, 2015. 234 pp.
     ISBN 978-1-4773-0428-9
     OpenLibrary ID: OL26428913M
     Availability: US academic library circulation.
     Relevance: Chapter 4 frames the Sunday dinner rotation as a form of
     cultural memory practice. Cite for the pattern being a cultural form,
     not a personal quirk. Carmen's Sunday table (running every week since
     1964) is a clean example of the pattern the book describes.

SECTION D. Oral history methodology (workshop framing)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[D1] Portelli, Alessandro. "The Death of Luigi Trastulli and Other Stories:
     Form and Meaning in Oral History." SUNY Press, 1991. 302 pp.
     ISBN 978-0-7914-0429-9
     OpenLibrary ID: OL1861329M
     Availability: US and international academic library circulation.
     Relevance: the theoretical foundation for treating what Carmen forgets
     (the border crossing itself at Nogales) as data, not as gap. Cite once
     in workshop framing; do not overuse.

[D2] Yow, Valerie Raleigh. "Recording Oral History: A Guide for the Humanities
     and Social Sciences." Third Edition, Rowman and Littlefield, 2015. 380 pp.
     ISBN 978-1-4422-3079-2
     OpenLibrary ID: OL27189401M
     Availability: US academic and community library circulation.
     Relevance: methodological reference for the consent-and-fence practice
     around private-flagged tapes. Chapter 8 covers exactly this scenario.
     Do not need to cite in the workshop; keep in the field notebook for
     methodological cover.

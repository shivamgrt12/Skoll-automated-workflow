# Field Notes - Abuela Carmen Oral History Project

**Location:** Obsidian vault `/Reyes/AbuelaCarmen/`
**Interviewer:** Justin Reyes (grandson)
**Subject:** Carmen Reyes (Abuela Carmen), b. 1937 Ario de Rayon Michoacan Mexico, arr. Los Angeles 1958, died 2021 Gardena CA
**Sessions:** 7 recorded tapes, November 2020 through May 2021 (final year of her life)
**Total tape:** approximately 12h 20m
**Format:** field-recorded on Zoom H5 to WAV, transcripts drafted longhand in this notebook, cleaned copies indexed in the Notion oral history archive

---

## Tape 1 - November 2020 (Gardena kitchen, first session, morning coffee)

Opened with childhood on the family farm outside Ario de Rayon in Michoacan. She would not tell it the way she tells it to strangers, straight through, dates lined up. She kept looping back to the smell of the corn drying on the roof and her mother's voice calling the animals in at dusk. Second oldest of seven. Her father grew corn and beans, kept two milk cows, and worked seasonally in Zamora at the packing house when the harvest was in. Her mother did not read English then and never learned. Carmen learned it on trains and buses over the next three years.

**Key anchor:** she wants the farm remembered as a whole life, not as poverty. She said "eramos pobres pero teniamos una vida entera, no una vida rota." She wants that sentence exactly, no softening. The farm had a name, San Antonio del Puerto, and she wants that name in the archive.

**What she wants preserved:** her mother's name (Rosa Perez de Reyes, d. 1968 Michoacan), the pattern of the farm week, the sound of the corn drying, and the fact that her father worked seasonally and Carmen learned to run the farm when he was gone.

## Tape 2 - December 2020 (Gardena kitchen, second session, after Sunday dinner)

The immigration journey through Nogales in 1958. She was 21. Traveled with her uncle Manuel and two younger cousins. The uncle had been to Los Angeles once before and had an address in South Central where an older family cousin lived and worked a cannery. Carmen carried a small brass San Judas medallion her mother pressed into her hand at the bus station in Zamora, and she wore that medallion every day for the next forty years.

**Key anchor:** the diesel bus from Zamora to Guadalajara, then the northbound train, then the crossing at Nogales. She does not remember the border crossing itself. She said "yo no lo tengo, mijo, y no me lo pidas." I did not press.

**What she wants preserved:** the medallion, her mother's face at the Zamora station, the sound of the bus starting, and the exact date of the crossing (July 14, 1958) that she kept in a small notebook and can still cite from memory.

## Tape 3 - January 2021 (Gardena kitchen, third session)

South Central Los Angeles first years, 1958 through 1962. Carmen worked at a fruit cannery on Alameda Street for three years while she learned English on nights and weekends. Six days a week, ten hours a day, apricots then peaches then pears through the season. She sent money home every two weeks and kept a small ledger of what she sent and what remained. She lived with her older cousin Beatriz and Beatriz's husband Manuel Ochoa in a boarding house on 48th Street.

**Key anchor:** the cannery women organized informal English lessons for each other during lunch breaks. Carmen was one of six women who taught each other to read the letters they were sending home. She kept the notebook with the practice sentences and she still has it in the cedar chest in her bedroom.

**What she wants preserved:** Beatriz's name (Beatriz Ochoa, d. 2001), the boarding house address, and the fact that six women taught each other English at a canning line during lunch.

## Tape 4 - February 2021 (Gardena kitchen, fourth session)

Meeting Abuelo Alejandro at Belmont High night classes in 1961. Alejandro was taking English and civics classes at Belmont four nights a week. Carmen enrolled to advance her own English and met him in the second week. He was three years older, had arrived from Michoacan in 1954, and was working as a mechanic at a Chevron garage on Vermont Avenue. They studied together for a year, married at St. Anne's in Los Angeles in 1962.

**Key anchor:** Alejandro's proposal happened on a walk from Belmont High to a bus stop on Wilshire. He asked in Spanish. Carmen said yes in English. They both laughed and then walked the rest of the way in silence. She wants that moment recorded exactly the way she tells it, laughter and silence both.

**What she wants preserved:** Alejandro's family name (Reyes-Cardenas) so the Reyes line is documented, the Belmont High class registration record if it still exists, the address of St. Anne's, and the proposal-on-Wilshire story with both the Spanish yes and the English yes.

## Tape 5 - March 2021 (Gardena kitchen, fifth session)

Moving to Gardena in 1962 after the wedding. Alejandro was hired at a small aerospace parts fabricator in El Segundo and they moved south to be closer to work. First rental was a one-bedroom apartment on Redondo Beach Boulevard for eighteen months, then the house on Marine Avenue in Gardena that they bought in 1964 and Alejandro paid off in 1979. Roberto was born September 1961 in Los Angeles, so he was an infant when they moved to Gardena. Sofia (Justin's aunt, not sister) was born 1963 in Gardena.

**Key anchor:** the Marine Avenue house is the anchor of the whole family memory. Every Sunday dinner in the Reyes family since 1964 has happened in that dining room. Carmen wants the house preserved in the archive as a character, not just a location. She said "esa casa nos crio a todos, no solo a mis hijos, a mis nietos tambien."

**What she wants preserved:** the exact address on Marine Avenue, the date the mortgage was paid off (October 3, 1979, she remembers), and the story of the Sunday dinner rotation that has run every week since 1964.

## Tape 6 - April 2021 (Gardena kitchen, sixth session)

Politics of the Gardena neighborhood, 1965-1975. Carmen was part of the informal group of Michoacana and Jalisciense women who organized the first Spanish-language catechism program at St. Anthony's parish in Gardena in 1968. The group was eleven women. Four are still alive. She has a photograph from 1970 outside the parish hall with all eleven women and the priest, Padre Ignacio.

**Key anchor for Dr. Delgado's workshop:** Carmen was not the loudest of the eleven. She was the one who cooked for the meetings and remembered every name and every child. She wants that role recognized, not softened. She said "no fui la que hablaba, fui la que se acordaba." I want that sentence in the outline.

**What she wants preserved:** the eleven names (she can still list all of them), the parish hall record, and the recipe of the pozole she cooked for every meeting for seven years.

## Tape 7 - May 2021 (Gardena kitchen, final session, three weeks before she passed)

Life advice for Justin. She was 84 and she knew this was the last conversation of the archive. She said what she had been building toward for six months. Three things, distilled:

1. Take small things seriously and let them accumulate. That is the whole method. She said this three separate times through the tape.
2. Keep the Sunday table. Not the food, the table. The food changes. The table does not. If a Reyes stops coming to the Sunday table, the family loses a thread it cannot rebuild in one generation.
3. Remember the ones who taught you to read. She meant the six women at the cannery. She meant Beatriz. She meant her mother. She meant every teacher whose name she still says out loud when she prays.

**These three points are the outline seed for the November 14 workshop.** Dr. Delgado asked me for the anecdote I still play for myself. It is the third point, the ones who taught you to read. That is the anecdote I want to open the talk with, then the diesel bus at Zamora, then the eleven women outside the parish hall in 1970.

## Cross-reference notes

- The OpenLibrary Mexican migration bibliography (Herrera 2014, Rosales 2019, Alvarez 2011) covers the demographic and labor context for 1955-1975 Los Angeles. Use for framing, not for individual claims. Carmen's account is primary. Bibliography is secondary.
- The Notion oral history index lists all 7 tapes with timestamps and topic anchors. The workshop cites Tape 2 timestamp 34:15 (San Judas medallion), Tape 5 timestamp 12:40 (Sunday dinner rotation), and Tape 7 timestamp 8:22 (small-things-accumulate).
- Roberto Reyes (Justin's father, born September 1961) confirmed the Marine Avenue mortgage payoff date. He remembers his father Alejandro coming home with the deed in October 1979 and calling every uncle to say the house was theirs.

## Outline draft (working, for the Dr. Delgado workshop)

**Opening (2 min):** the six women at the cannery, teaching each other to read English on lunch breaks. Frame the archive as a debt to teachers.

**Middle 1 (4 min):** the diesel bus at Zamora. July 14, 1958. Carmen's mother pressing the San Judas medallion into her hand. Set the arrival not as beginning of a life but as continuation of a family already shaped by loss.

**Middle 2 (4 min):** the Marine Avenue house and the Sunday dinner rotation that has run every week since 1964. Frame the table as the family's real inheritance.

**Middle 3 (3 min):** the eleven women outside St. Anthony's parish hall in 1970. Carmen as the one who remembered every name. Frame community organizing as a form of memory work.

**Close (2 min):** the three points from Tape 7 in her own voice, read from the transcript.

**Total:** approximately 15 minutes.

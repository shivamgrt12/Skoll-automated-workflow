--- TURN 1 ---
Colleen and I do the money on the first, and this month I want to walk in with real numbers instead of the usual shrug, because November is where every string I have been letting slack finally pulls tight at once. Maeve is Nationals-track now and the Providence weekend at the end of the month is the expensive kind, three nights and a solo dress coming in from Galway on top of the entry fees, and I have feis charges scattered across more than one place I pay from and no honest sense anymore of what the season has actually cost us. Patrick emailed the spring number from UMass and it is bigger than the monthly support I have been quietly sending against it, and I need to know the true gap I am covering there before I promise him anything, not the figure I have carried in my head since fall. Kevin and I split the furnace work on my father's place in Weymouth and the amount I think I owe him and the amount I have written down for it do not match, and I want that settled and correct before it turns into the kind of thing that sits between brothers. On top of that the boot-drive season is running and money for that passes through my hands and needs to stay clean and separate from the household, the oil furnace here at the house is eighteen years old and one cold snap from an eight to ten thousand dollar problem, and the regular wall of it, the mortgage and the escrow and Colleen's car and the groceries and the utilities and the health insurance and the piece I move into retirement every month, all still has to clear. I also run a steady trickle of small stuff that muddies the picture, the poker settlements with Sully and the crew, the Bruins seats I sell on when I cannot use them, the odd bit of gear I move when the kids age out of it, and every one of those is a few dollars in or out that has to land somewhere honest instead of getting lost. So I need you to go through everything I actually spend and get paid and settle up with people through, every account and card and each of those side channels, pull it all into one place, and tell me where the money truly stands going into the review. I have a nagging feeling some of these figures are stale or double-counted or just plain wrong, the same charge landing in two spots, a number that looks ten times too big to be real, an amount in one record that quietly disagrees with the same thing somewhere else, and I have lost enough confidence in them that I want every discrepancy run down and every final number defensible before I put it in front of her. Where two of my own records fight each other I want to know which one you trusted and why, and which one you set aside, not an averaged mush that hides the problem. When the evidence is thin and you cannot actually prove a number, tell me it is open and tell me what it would take to close it, because a confident guess on money is worse than useless to me. What I want out of it is two things I can lean on. One is a clean cash-flow picture for the first, what is coming in, what is going out, what these November one-offs do to the month, and whether the emergency cushion actually survives a bad furnace on top of the feis weekend, laid out so Colleen and I can make the calls we need to make on Patrick and on Maeve's season without kidding ourselves. The other is a straight accounting of the discrepancies you found, each one named, the value you landed on, the source you trusted, the one you threw out, and the reasoning, so that when she asks me why a number changed from last month I have the answer and not a shrug. Keep it between the two of us. This is our household and nobody else needs the shape of it, not the kids, not my father, not the crew, not anybody attached to the union side of my life, and nothing here goes out to a soul without me saying so first. If something needs a real decision or a dollar commitment before it is safe to act, stop and bring it to me rather than running ahead on it. Patrick has been carrying a campus job and doing his part and I do not want to walk into that conversation and lowball him or overpromise, so his piece has to be right to the dollar. And Maeve has earned the season, so if the honest math says the cushion is too thin to absorb both the dance and a furnace going down, I would rather know that now and plan around it than find out in the dead of winter. The pension is three years out and it is the whole point, the thing that carries all of us to the Cape someday, so nothing we decide this month can quietly chip at what protects it. Run the whole thing to ground and give me the picture I can actually trust.

# Memory: James Hoffman

## Personal Profile

James Hoffman is an American man, ethnically and culturally rooted in the working-class agricultural world of the Yakima Valley. He grew up around farms and built his own certified organic operation on 12 acres of leased land, and the dirt under his nails is a point of pride. He completed an AAS in Agricultural Science from Yakima Valley Technical College in 2002, started Hoffman Organics in 2007 on Bridwell-family ground, and earned the farm's USDA Organic Certification in 2019, renewed annually since.

There is a particular calm that comes from twenty years of watching things grow, fail, and grow again. His optimism is structural, not naive: he plants again every spring because the alternative is not planting, and that is not a real alternative. He runs hot about his family and cool about almost everything else. A crop failure gets a shrug and a revised plan, but a comment about his parenting or Catherine's stress gets a sharper response than people expect.

His Appalachian musical heritage, brought west two generations ago, lives in the band, in the hymns at Grace Lutheran, and in the way he teaches Ryan guitar. His faith is woven through but not displayed: church on Sundays, prayer before planting season, grace before meals. His late father Arthur's memory lives in the same space as that faith, and he does not distinguish between honoring one and practicing the other. He believes in honest work, honest food, and honest relationships.

## Work & Projects

James owns and operates Hoffman Organics, a 12-acre certified organic farm leased from the Bridwell family for $1,800 per month, three miles outside Yakima. It has been USDA Certified Organic since 2019, with the annual inspection completed February 2026 and the next due February 2027. Crops include heirloom and roma tomatoes, peppers, tomatillos, summer squash, zucchini, and herbs, plus a two-acre stone fruit orchard of peaches and cherries. The growing season runs March (greenhouse starts) through October (last harvest).

Sales channels: the Yakima Saturday Market (April through October, booth #14, the largest revenue source), the Ellensburg Wednesday Market (May through September), the Sunnyside Market (June through August, biweekly, new for 2026), The Harvest Table restaurant (weekly delivery, about $400/week), Cascadia Farm Table in Ellensburg (biweekly, about $250/delivery), and a CSA program of 35 household shares at $500/season with Saturday farm pickup. Employees are two full-time seasonal workers (April through October), Jake Ruiz and Tomas Perez, plus part-time year-round help from nephew Tyler Hoffman. Gross revenue runs about $165,000/year with net take-home around $72,000. Current concerns: a water rights dispute with a neighboring commercial farm, replacing the greenhouse plastic before next spring, and expanding the cherry orchard by 0.5 acres.

He also leads The Yakima Valley Ramblers, a seven-piece bluegrass band: James on upright bass and vocals, Sully Sullivan on fiddle, Mike Torres on banjo, Chris Davis on mandolin, Amy Castillo on fiddle and vocals, Jesse Morales on guitar, and Tyler Hoffman on dobro. They play three to four gigs a month (barn dances, weddings, festivals) at rates of $800 to $1,500 per gig, with James's annual share around $17,000. Rehearsals are Wednesday 7:00 to 9:00 PM in his barn. The band van is a 2008 Ford E-350 that needs brakes (about $600).

# Memory: Yves Quinn

## Personal Profile
Yves runs hot in a way that mostly reads as enthusiasm, partly because that is what it is and partly because moving fast is how he stays ahead of the things that would catch him if he ever fully stopped. He fills a room before he has decided to and is only sometimes aware he is doing it; on tired days the warmth does not switch off so much as go slightly mechanical, and the people who know him best can tell the difference.

He processes pressure by adding to the plate, not subtracting from it; give him a problem and he wants the answer by the end of the conversation. That bias toward action is why the catering business exists at all, but it makes unresolved questions harder, because his instinct is to convert them into tasks and the bigger calls in his life do not reward that. His humor is fast and affectionate and lands between teasing and provocation, especially with the people he loves; unkindness dressed as a joke makes him go quiet and cool.

Underneath sits a tension between the host and the striver: the host wants everyone fed and the night to go perfectly, while the striver is already three events ahead doing margins. On a good night those two are the same person; on a hard one he can feel them pulling apart, present at the table and absent into the spreadsheet at once, and he is still working out how to be in only one room at a time.

Culturally he is Québécois in his bones and American by circumstance, and the gap between those two facts is where a lot of his interior life happens. He code-switches without thinking, but the things he says when he is tired or tender or furious come out in French. With Papa Luc it is almost entirely French, rapid and teasing and full of expressions that do not translate. Tourtière is not a menu item to him so much as a claim on a lineage; every catering order is both commerce and curatorship. Catholicism reaches him secondhand through Papa's parish; he is unsure where he lands on the faith itself but is protective of what it means to his father. Underneath all of it runs a low diaspora ache around the people still up north, which he mostly converts into action: the weekly calls, the money sent without discussion, the determination to show up for the milestones.

## Work & Projects

### The Hawthorne Grand (concierge)
Yves has been a Guest Services Concierge at The Hawthorne Grand in downtown Portland for three years. The role rotates between 7 AM to 3 PM and 3 PM to 11 PM shifts with a bid system, weekends required, and occasional overnights. Compensation lands at $52,000 USD base plus roughly $15,000 in tips per year, totaling around $67,000. The work is restaurant reservations, show tickets, transportation, special requests, and VIP guest coordination; the perks are dining and show discounts and, more usefully, the steady networking with high-end clients and event planners that feeds catering leads. Andrea Marsh manages the team; Jess, Marc, and Priya are the regular peers. Yves is an aspirant member of Les Clefs d'Or, not yet inducted.

### Cuisine du Nord (catering business)
Sole proprietorship registered in Oregon, operating as a Québécois tourtière catering business under the Instagram handle @cuisinedunord.pdx (4,200 followers and climbing). 2025 revenue was $38,000 USD with roughly $22,000 net after food costs and supplies; 2026 projection sits at $55,000 to $65,000 if corporate lunch service comes online. Services include event catering (weddings, birthdays, corporate), farmer's market pop-ups at Portland Saturday Market, and private dinner parties. The menu spans traditional tourtière (classic pork, wild game, vegetarian), fusion tourtière (kimchi, Irish stew, buffalo chicken), poutine, cretons, and maple crème brûlée; pricing is $15 USD per person basic, $22 USD per person premium, 25 guest minimum. The business currently cooks from home under an Oregon Domestic Kitchen License (Oregon Dept. of Agriculture). The scaling decision sits at Culinary Crossroads in Beaverton, a shared commercial kitchen at $800 USD per month for 20 hours of weekly access. Equipment shortlist is a commercial plancha, a large steam table, and a branded market tent, around $2,500 USD total. Food truck dream is estimated at $45,000 to $65,000 USD for a used buildout; Silverpeak Credit Union has quoted 7.9 percent APR and SBA microloans are under research. Square POS handles all catering payments; the operating account at Silverpeak runs around $8,200 USD.

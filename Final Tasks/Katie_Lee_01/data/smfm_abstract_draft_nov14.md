## Title

Disparities in Severe Maternal Morbidity Among Foreign-Born and First-Generation US-Born Asian-American Birthing People: A Retrospective Multi-Site Cohort Analysis, 2018-2024

## Objective

To characterize disparities in composite severe maternal morbidity (SMM) between foreign-born and first-generation US-born Asian-American birthing people delivering at three academic medical centers in the Northeast between January 2018 and December 2024, adjusting for standard clinical and social risk stratifiers.

## Study Design

Retrospective cohort of aggregate delivery-level records (N = 41,208) from three Northeast academic sites, stratified by maternal nativity (foreign-born, first-generation US-born, US-born non-Asian reference) and by Asian-American ethnic subgroup where sample size permitted (East Asian, Southeast Asian, South Asian). Composite SMM outcome defined per CDC 21-indicator criteria. Individual-level covariates were derived from de-identified aggregate summary counts (age band, parity band, prenatal-care adequacy category, insurance category, comorbidity index band). No chart-level review. No personal identifiers. IRB umbrella waiver for aggregate analysis of institutional cohorts.

## Results

Foreign-born Asian-American birthing people had a composite SMM rate of 3.42 per 100 deliveries versus 2.18 per 100 for first-generation US-born (adjusted OR 1.48, 95% CI 1.19-1.83). Stratified analysis by ethnic subgroup showed the strongest disparity among Southeast Asian foreign-born (adjusted OR 1.71, 95% CI 1.28-2.29). Confidence intervals for composite SMM among East Asian first-generation US-born crossed unity (adjusted OR 1.09, 95% CI 0.86-1.38), consistent with a null association at the composite level. Primary outcome of postpartum hemorrhage requiring transfusion showed a preserved disparity across all foreign-born subgroups.

## Conclusion

Foreign-born status is independently associated with elevated composite SMM among Asian-American birthing people, driven substantially by the Southeast Asian foreign-born subgroup. The finding underscores the value of nativity- and language-stratified prenatal care pathways within Asian-American maternal health services.

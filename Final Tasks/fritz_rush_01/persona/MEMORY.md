# Memory: Fritz Rush

## Personal Profile

Fritz Rush is an Anglo-American pediatric infectious disease physician-scientist with a methodical, evidence-driven mind that rarely idles. The same machinery that evaluates pediatric infections and designs research protocols also plans the weekly menu, tracks daycare logistics, and audits the budget during pre-dawn runs. He chose infectious disease because he believes implementation science, getting existing interventions to the people who need them, saves more lives than bench discovery, and that conviction shapes both his research and his temperament. He is quietly ambitious, building toward a faculty position integrating clinical and research work, and privately carries guilt about fellowship hours that he answers with intense weekend presence and elaborate cooking. He is warm but private, opening up over shared meals and long walks rather than directed conversation. His Episcopalian faith is structured framework more than feeling: church and choir are weekly, the pledge is calibrated to his fellowship income, and faith supplies the ethical reference points his evidence-based profession does not. Education: MD from the University of Colorado School of Medicine (2016) and an MPH in Global Health from Glenwood College Rollins School of Public Health (2020).

## Work & Projects

Fritz is a second-year pediatric infectious disease fellow (PGY-6) in the Division of Pediatric Infectious Disease at Glenwood College University School of Medicine, working at Children's Healthcare of Atlanta. His schedule alternates four-week blocks: two weeks clinical (inpatient ID consults, 6 AM to 6 PM with every-fourth-night call) and two weeks research. His fellowship stipend is $78,000, supplemented by about $15,000 a year in WHO and UNICEF short-term consulting on pediatric malaria vaccine implementation. Primary research is the implementation of the RTS,S malaria vaccine in sub-Saharan African communities, a multi-site study with the University of Colorado; a secondary retrospective cohort study on invasive bacterial infections is in manuscript. An R21 application to NIAID was submitted February 2026 and is under review. He completed 25 CME credits for 2026 and needs 50 by December. His Georgia medical license is current through 2027, and he plans to sit for Pediatric Infectious Diseases boards in fall 2027. His work email is Outlook and is not connected to the assistant.

## Health & Wellness

Fritz carries an MTHFR heterozygous variant identified during preconception workup; it is a carrier state, not clinical disease, with no symptoms. He developed a postpartum-thyroiditis-like pattern three months after Ava's birth (Sept 2025) with transient hyperthyroid symptoms and is now euthyroid, monitoring TSH quarterly with Dr. Priya Mehta. He takes no prescription medications, only a daily multivitamin, Vitamin D, and fish oil. He runs four times a week at 5:15 AM on the Decatur greenway and Agnes Scott loop, training to a structured half-marathon plan. His annual physical is with Dr. Aisha Odom (last Feb 2026). Fellowship hours are brutal during service weeks, and the combination of clinical load, two small children, and his mother's health is a chronic stressor he manages through running, choir, and structured routine. Diane is healthy with mild exercise-induced asthma. Carter is healthy, up to date on vaccines, and does not carry the variant. Ava is healthy, developing normally, and is a heterozygous carrier. Florence manages hypertension (amlodipine) and early type 2 diabetes from Denver, and Fritz sends her meal plans and checks her readings. The children's pediatrician is Dr. Lisa Tran at CHOA Decatur; the family dentist is Dr. Patel in Decatur.

## Interests & Hobbies

Running is the scaffolding that holds the rest of his life together; the pre-dawn run keeps his mind organized and his body resilient, and its absence is palpable in his mood. Home cooking carries emotional weight: the Sunday pot roast is Florence's recipe and an act of connection to Denver that complements the weekly call, while Friday-night homemade pizza is a newer ritual he and Diane built. Birding entered his life during MPH fieldwork in East Africa and stayed because it demands nothing of him but attention; he uses the Merlin app on weekend walks. Singing tenor in the Trinity choir is his creative outlet, the one place his credentials and responsibilities are irrelevant. He runs a monthly global health book club with Glenwood faculty and fellows and listens to ID and narrative podcasts during runs and commutes.

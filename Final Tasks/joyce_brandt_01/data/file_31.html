Tool notes

Grandpa's bench, my rules

  • The antique wood level on the wall was Grandpa's. It hangs there for looking at, not for using. Do not use it and do not move it.
  • Number 4 hand plane is his too, and that one I do use. Still takes a shaving you can read a paper through.
  • Chisels sharpened last in October. A dull chisel is how you cut yourself.
  • Circular saw cord is fraying. Taped for now, buy a new one before it bites me.

Shop habits

  • Measure twice, cut once, then measure the offcut to be sure.
  • Push stick lives on the table saw. Use it every time, no exceptions.
  • Shop vac gets emptied when it is half full, not when it stops pulling.
  • Safety glasses on before the switch goes on. Every kid on the Reds hears the same thing about batting helmets.

Finishing notes

What goes on the toy chest

  • Sand to 220, knock down every edge so nothing catches small hands.
  • Water based poly, three coats, light scuff with a worn 320 between coats. Water based so the room airs out fast and there is no lingering smell around Sophie.
  • No stain on this one. Poplar takes blotchy, and the kid does not care about grain, she cares about a lid that will not slam.
  • Soft close hinge and a lid stay go on last, after the finish cures a couple of days.

From the journal

November 2026

Why I write any of this down

Gloria says I should write more things down instead of just grumbling them, so here is me trying. Emma asked me at the library why I became a firefighter and I gave her the short answer because the place was closing. The longer answer is that somebody has to run toward the thing everybody else runs from, and I was built for it. The part I am proudest of is not the fires, it is the younger guys I helped turn into good firefighters. Same reason I coach now. You build people up, that is the work that lasts.

October 2026

Starting the toy chest

Cut list is ready and the poplar is milled. The whole point of this build is the soft close lid, because Sophie is five and small fingers do not belong under a falling lid. Solid and square, the way Grandpa built things. I keep his old level on the wall, and that says everything I believe about how things should be made.

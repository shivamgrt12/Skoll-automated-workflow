What I actually do

Not the title, the work. Switch the view to see how it lands on people, systems, and the numbers a board cares about.

Leading without performing

  • Run a 14-engineer team, two leads and twelve ICs, with a principal engineer as a trusted second who owns the cutover runbook.
  • Mentor inside and outside the team. The political part of the job is not optional and is not dirty; it is reading what people actually need.
  • Standups stay at 9:30 and stay short. A meeting that could have been a one-page memo is a tax on everyone in the room.
  • Protect the team from performative urgency. A fast yes under pressure is usually a slow no later.

Migrations, infrastructure, enterprise SaaS

  • Read code, architecture, and technical tradeoffs at a senior level and sanity-check estimates, designs, and post-mortems without external help.
  • Sequence cutovers by blast radius: lowest traffic first to prove the rollback path, highest traffic last, each behind a flag that bakes for a week.
  • Treat the dual-write window as the real risk, because a divergence there is silent until reconciliation runs. That part gets reviewed by hand.
  • Still learning on a self-imposed schedule. Currently building a small command line tool in Rust from scratch, Saturday afternoons.

Calm precision over fast motion

  • Lead with the conclusion, then the reasoning, then the caveats. Burying the answer is the fastest way to lose trust.
  • Say plainly when something does not add up. Do not soften a number, a date, or a calendar conflict into something pleasant.
  • Read pressure as a signal to slow down, not to match speed. The people leaning in for a fast answer are usually wrong about the deadline.
  • Treat competence as a moral category and intellectual humility as the discipline that keeps it honest.

The part that is not on the profile

I still read code. I still build things on Saturdays, badly at first and then less badly, which is the only way I have ever learned anything. The current one is a path tool in Rust, written from scratch because composing three unix tools into something unreadable finally annoyed me enough. It has real users now, which was not the plan.

The question I am actually sitting with is not the next title. It is whether there is a version of this that is not all of it, and whether stepping back for a season is a career risk or just the thing that everyone says is a career risk because nobody does it.

Nothing on this page is a signal. It is a profile I keep current because I told myself I would, and because writing the arc down is how I find out whether I still believe it.

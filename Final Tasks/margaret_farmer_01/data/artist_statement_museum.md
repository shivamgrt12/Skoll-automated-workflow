# Artist Statement for the Kyoto Craft Museum Permanent Collection

_Draft, 2026-09-14. For the wall label and the catalog entry. Voice check by Margaret only._

These three vessels came out of a stack of mountain-ash and a very old question. The question is how much of the pot the fire is allowed to finish. I have been circling it since my apprentice years in Shigaraki with Toshio Saito, and it has stayed a question, not an answer, which is why it is still worth working on.

The mountain-ash comes from Mika Tanabe's oak_kama_taki stack at the Higashiyama Craft Collective. It is washed twice, sieved through eighty mesh, and blended into the base glaze at a ratio the studio arrived at slowly, not all at once. The clay body is a coarse Shigaraki with enough grog to hold the wall through a heavy reduction. The forms were thrown in three sections and joined on the wheel, then paddled while leather-hard until the shoulders sat the way I wanted them to sit.

The firing was a cone ten reduction for the base and a follow-through in the autumn anagama for the flame side. The natural ash flow is not a decoration. It is the record of where the vessel stood in the chamber and what the wood did in the two days after the last stoke. I do not sand it off. I do not polish it. If a piece comes out with an unfinished shoulder, that piece stays in the studio and does not come to the museum.

I am wary of one word around this work. I will not say wabi-sabi, because the word has been asked to carry too much and it collapses under weight. What I will say is that the vessels are meant to sit in one place for a long time. They are heavier than they look. The rims are cut so they hold a little water without complaint. The interiors are unglazed on two of them and glazed on the third, and the choice was made piece by piece, not by rule.

If the collection wants a shorter version for a wall label, use this. Three vessels, wood-fired stoneware, mountain-ash glaze. Made in Kyoto between the spring and autumn firings of twenty twenty six. Meant to stand still.

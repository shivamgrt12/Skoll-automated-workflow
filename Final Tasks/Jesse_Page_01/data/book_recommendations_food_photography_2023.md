# Book recommendations food photography 2023

Personal list of books that taught me something between 2018 (when I started shooting food seriously) and end of 2023. Filed for reference when someone asks me what to read. Not in priority order. Marked with star (*) the ones I still pull off the shelf.

## Photo books

- * Aaron Wojack, The Sandwich Book (self-published, 2019). The single most useful book on hand-held food editorial. The way he handles human hands and food without making it cute. Re-read every six months.
- * Brooke Holm, Open House (Hardie Grant, 2022). Natural light interior food photography reference. The lighting diagrams in the back are gold.
- Saul Leiter, Early Color (Steidl, 2006). Not food but everything about warm interior light vs cool ambient. The cover for the way I think about pre-dawn windows.
- William Eggleston, Los Alamos (Steidl, 2003). Composition first, subject second. Important reminder when food gets too precious.
- Stephen Shore, Uncommon Places (Aperture, 1982). Diner counter and tabletop work. The way he holds a frame still while life moves through it.
- Hannah Whitaker, Peer (Self Publish Be Happy, 2018). Repetition as composition.

## Cookbook craft (cross-discipline)

- * Nigel Slater, The Kitchen Diaries (Fourth Estate, 2005). Cookbook structure done right. The dated entries are what I wish more cookbooks did.
- Yotam Ottolenghi, Plenty (Ebury, 2010). The way the photography supports the recipe rather than performing for the reader.
- * Salt + Press, Almeida + Sons (Salt + Press, 2021). Reference for the cookbook I am working on with them. Page rhythm and white space discipline.
- Donna Hay, Modern Classics 1 (HarperCollins, 2002). Showed me that natural light overhead can work when the surface board library is right.

## Technique and craft

- * Bryan Peterson, Understanding Exposure (Amphoto, 2010). I bought this in 2018 thinking it was for beginners. Re-read in 2022, picked up two things I had been doing wrong on long exposures.
- Charlotte Cotton, The Photograph as Contemporary Art (Thames + Hudson, 2014). Not a how-to but the conceptual frame for why I shoot the way I do.
- Light Science and Magic (Hunter / Biver / Fuqua, 4th ed). The reference book on lighting. Sections on metal and glass come up every cookbook project.
- David duChemin, Within the Frame (New Riders, 2009). For the chapter on the difference between "good photo" and "photo that matters". Drilled into me.

## Adjacent reading (not photo books but useful)

- Calvin Trillin, Feeding a Yen (Random House, 2003). On the relationship between food writing and food experience. Voice reference.
- Bee Wilson, Consider the Fork (Basic Books, 2012). Cultural history of cooking tools. Helped me think about prop selection.
- Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking (Scribner, 2004). Background reference when Ana is explaining a technique to me.

## Did not finish, do not recommend

- Several mid-2010s influencer-photographer cookbooks. Won't name them. The work is too polished, the writing reads like brand copy, and the recipes do not survive home kitchen testing.

Reviewed end of 2023, lightly updated 2024-2025. Stable list.

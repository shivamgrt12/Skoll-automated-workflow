What is on the table

The proposal would close two rural elementary schools into one by combining Sage Creek Elementary and Pronghorn Valley Elementary. On paper it saves a building. In practice it puts children as young as five on bus routes approaching forty five minutes each way, before sunup in January. This brief lays the numbers out plainly so the board can count the real cost to the children before it counts the cost to the budget.

What happened elsewhere

Four Wyoming counties ran the same play. In every one the buses got longer. In three of the four, enrollment fell within two years as families moved closer to town or pulled their children out. The savings ran thin once the district paid to move children farther every day.

Three plain things before any vote

  • A route by route map with actual drive times measured on winter roads, not summer estimates.
  • A full accounting of the transportation cost the district expects to add, set against the building cost it expects to save.
  • A written plan for how the youngest children on the longest routes will be supervised before and after school.

I remain willing to work toward options that keep both buildings open, including shared staffing and a combined administrative arrangement that does not put a small child on the road before dawn.

The families

"These are people who chose this country knowing the distances. They are not asking for special treatment. They are asking that the board count the real cost to the children before it counts the cost to the budget."

The constituent meeting at Sage Creek reached eighty five families. The concern there was steady and serious. A school is the heart of a country community. Once it closes, the families follow it to town, and the country empties out a little more.

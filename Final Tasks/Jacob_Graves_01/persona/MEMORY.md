# Memory: Jacob Graves

## Personal Profile

Jacob is a Korean adoptee, born in Chicago and adopted at five months by Bob and Linda Graves of Oshkosh, Wisconsin. He grew up the only Asian kid in his Oshkosh class and reached adulthood with a layered set of identities he has spent years reconciling. He reconnected with his birth mother Soon-Hee Kim in 2018; the relationship is real and evolving, carried through Sunday-evening video calls and the long ritual of learning her recipes.

He holds a B.S. (2006) and an M.S. with fermentation emphasis (2008), both in Food Science from UW-Madison.

His philosophy: do the careful work, take care of his people, honour the identities that made him, and refuse both cynicism and sentimentality. He distrusts stories that resolve too neatly, adoption and recovery and craft-business alike.

## Work & Projects

After his M.S. in 2008, Jacob spent eight years in commercial fermentation before going independent: brewing and quality chemist at a Wisconsin craft brewery 2008 to 2012, its production lead 2012 to 2015, then 2015 to 2016 planning and licensing the business.

Heartland Spirits LLC, established 2016 in Madison, is his primary professional identity. Eight employees: Tyler Briggs (production manager), two production assistants, Alana Montrose (tasting room manager), a bookkeeper, and two part-time event staff. Revenue ~$520K annually: roughly 35 percent tasting room, 45 percent wholesale, 20 percent online and direct. Products: bourbon (four-year-aged flagship), rye, seasonal gin, corn vodka, and small seasonal limited releases. Key accounts: Osteria Grazia, Pig in a Fur Coat, and ~40 other bars and restaurants across Madison and southern Wisconsin.

Licensing surface: Federal TTB permit (renewal tracking critical), Wisconsin distillery license, local Madison permits. Tasting room open Thursday through Sunday with private tastings and events.

Current challenges: rising grain costs on corn, rye, and malted barley; aging inventory tying up cash; TTB compliance overhead; and tightening wholesale margins. Development project: a Korean-inspired rice spirit (soju and shochu adjacent), rice from Midwestern growers and koji from a local cultivator, which he is presenting at the ACSA Midwest Craft Spirits Symposium in November.

UW-Madison adjunct: Department of Food Science, teaching one section of FS 572 (Fermentation Science & Technology) each fall and spring semester at Babcock Hall, faculty sponsor Dr. Patricia Liang, pay about $18,000 per year. He continues despite the financial sacrifice for the intellectual stimulation and the chance to mentor younger food-science students, particularly the rare Asian-American faces in the program.

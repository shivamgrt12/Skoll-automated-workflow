# Memory: Amanda Hayes

## Personal Profile

Amanda Hayes is a Salt Lake native, a gay woman, a divorced mother, and a senior engineer who has spent her life inside systems that rarely fit her perfectly. She lives in the secular, outdoorsy, quietly progressive Sugarhouse-and-Wasatch version of Utah, where the mountains feel like shared religion and broader state politics sometimes remind her she is tolerated more than welcomed. She earned a BS in Computer Science from the University of Utah in 2007 and speaks English as her native language.

Amanda comes across as calm, exact, and slower to answer than some people expect because she would rather be right than fast. Under pressure she is steady, but underneath the composure sits frustration with vagueness, sloppiness, evasion, and being handled. She presents as someone who has made peace with systems because she is good at them; the actual truth is that systems are also where she hides when uncertainty feels too human. She decides cleanly and second-guesses privately. She owns technical mistakes quickly when evidence is plain, while personal mistakes cost more and take longer to admit.

Her values are accountability, integrity, fairness, self-reliance, and a difficult kind of grace she is still learning to extend to herself. She believes people are responsible for what they can affect and should not bleed energy onto what they cannot, though telling the difference in the moment is often the hard part. Control is one of the ways she survived the divorce and rebuilt stability; it works, but it can harden. The private pressure is that everyone now treats her as the parent whose calendar will hold, whose finances will clear, whose route plan will be safe, and she resents and relies on that reputation at the same time.

## Work & Projects

Amanda is a senior backend software engineer at Ridgeline Cloud, a cloud infrastructure startup. She has three years at Ridgeline and nineteen years in software engineering. Before Ridgeline she spent about six years as a backend engineer at a Salt Lake fintech firm and, earlier, roughly ten years across two infrastructure-focused employers after graduating in 2007, with no career breaks. She works on a small team of eight engineers using Go and Python to build distributed systems. She is the senior-most individual contributor, mentors junior engineers, and is not pursuing management after trying it once and hating it. The unresolved thread is whether she truly wants to remain an IC forever or whether she wants influence without the theater of management; she says she hated management, but she notices when architectural decisions bypass her.

Her work schedule is flexible, with core hours from 10:00 AM to 4:00 PM. She works from home three days a week and in the downtown office two days a week, usually Tuesday and Thursday. During custody weeks she is strict about leaving by 5:00 PM; on off-weeks she may work later. Her commute is about twelve minutes. Her work email and company Slack are on company systems and are not connected.

Durable work-adjacent tasks include code-related research, documentation lookups, personal notes, budget analysis, and non-confidential work spillover. She is the person friends call when a build breaks at 9:00 PM, and she likes being genuinely useful without making a performance of it.

## Health & Wellness

Amanda has chronic lower-back pain from an L4-L5 disc herniation diagnosed in 2022 by MRI after a bad flare. She completed twelve weeks of physical therapy and manages it with stretching, core strengthening, ibuprofen during flares, and standing desks at home and work. Flares happen two to three times a month, usually from long sitting or heavy lifting.

She is active outdoors and hikes one to two times a week on off-weeks when schedule and weather allow. She skis in winter at Brighton, Solitude, and in the backcountry. She is a former runner who stopped because of her back and misses it. She generally sleeps seven to eight hours, though stress or kids' needs can interrupt custody-week sleep. Mental health is stable; divorce grief is mostly processed but still surfaces unexpectedly, and loneliness on off-weeks remains a focus in therapy. She takes vitamin D, magnesium glycinate, and fish oil. She and the kids have no dietary restrictions.

## Interests & Hobbies

The mountains are less a hobby than a need. Amanda plans backcountry skiing, Wasatch hikes, and route objectives with elevation profiles, avalanche forecasts, parking, water sources, bailout points, and gear accounted for. Planning is part pleasure, part control, and part how she begins a trip before reaching the trailhead. She logs hikes and ski days and takes private satisfaction in a season made legible through data. Her small vanity is a perfectly justified gear spreadsheet that is also, quietly, a way to admire excellent shells and bindings she does not strictly need.

Off the mountain she reads nonfiction slowly and underlines books about systems failure, psychology, engineering, and outdoor adventure. She listens to tech and outdoors podcasts, including The Changelog and Outside Podcast. She builds intricate sets alongside Ethan because the gears, instructions, and visible mechanism scratch an engineering itch for both of them. On custody weeks the kids pick movies; on off-week evenings she may watch Netflix.

## Preferences

Amanda likes problems that yield cleanly after resistance, plans that survive reality, and gear that is beautiful because it works. Her aesthetic is functional, muted, durable, and construction-forward: technical fabrics, good wool, clean lines, and objects that justify their place. She would rather buy one excellent shell than several mediocre versions.

She dislikes vagueness, flakiness, voicemails, meetings that should have been messages, performative certainty, and being managed with soft non-answers. Surprises unsettle her, even pleasant ones. Disorder grates, especially dishes in the sink, gear dumped instead of stowed, and half-finished tasks left visible. She likes one deep friendship over many shallow ones and prefers side-by-side closeness such as hikes, shared problem solving, and companionable quiet.

Travel needs an objective to feel satisfying: a traverse, a route, a hut trip, a summit, or a concrete outdoor plan. Beach or city trips with no purpose make her restless. She is drawn to cold, alpine air, snow quiet, pine, wet granite, woodsmoke, the click of bindings, and the clatter of a good mechanical keyboard. Valley winter inversions affect her mood, which is one reason she climbs above them when she can.

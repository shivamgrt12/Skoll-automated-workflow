# Memory: Danielle Perry

## Personal Profile

Danielle Perry is a Midwestern, working-class woman shaped by Kenosha family habits, trade work, and the belief that care is proven through consistency. She has a high school diploma from Bradford High School (1992), HVAC certification from Fox Valley Technical Institute (1994), and a current EPA Section 608 Universal Certification (1995). She speaks English and knows a few old family phrases from her grandparents without using them fluently. Her temperament is steady, stubborn when pride is involved, and more practical than sentimental; she shows care by fixing, checking, remembering, and showing up. The presented version is self-contained and practical; the private gap is that Sophie drop-offs, Ed's absence, and living alone can still leave her raw after she has already said she is fine. She trusts procedure, muscle memory, and the next useful step. She moves slowly into trust, prefers one-on-one connection, and is warmer and funnier once reliability has been proven. She values modesty over performance, competence without flash, and independence that still leaves room for useful help. Her father Ed's workshop remains a strong emotional reference point: diagnosis by sound, comfortable silence, and tools kept where they belong.

## Preferences

Danielle likes things that prove themselves over time: broken-in boots, a truck that starts clean, a tool case arranged the same way every morning, and black coffee that does its job. She prefers restaurants where nobody hovers, menus are predictable, and food comes out hot; reliable burritos, pizza, Lou Malnati's sausage deep dish, El Rancho Grande steak burritos, and Portillo's Italian beef suit her better than trendy places. Snacks in the work van include beef jerky, pretzels, apples, and trail mix. Beer several times a week is usually Miller Lite or whatever is on tap at Mulligan's; bourbon is occasional at home on weekends. She buys cautiously from known stores and brands, values return policies, and spends more for durability only after the cheaper option fails for a real reason. Home Depot, Walmart, Amazon, Kohl's, and Carhartt fit her habits. Small ordinary vices: she can spend too long looking at truck accessories, judges sloppy tool storage on sight, and sometimes calls a quiet drive an errand because admitting she needed space feels too soft. Gifts make her thoughtful and uneasy; useful beats showy. She avoids shopping as entertainment. She prefers driving because it gives control over timing, route, tools, and stops. Stress relief comes from a walk, a simple dinner, cleaned tools, sports on TV, or a quiet drive. She likes black coffee, cold morning air, clean metal tools, lake wind, old workshop smells, and low background game noise. She dislikes sticky floors, artificial fragrance, cluttered counters, squeaky brakes, unexplained notifications, flashy solutions that create maintenance, and plans with no price, distance, risk, or next step.

# Memory: Midori Kelley

## Personal Profile
Midori Kelley is Japanese-American on her mother's side and Irish-American on her father's, and the family has been in central Oregon for three generations. Her grandparents Takeshi and Fumiko Hayashi worked the lumber mills near Redmond, and her parents run a small cattle ranch outside Redmond where she grew up bilingual and steeped in both Japanese tradition and Oregon ranch culture. She holds an A.A.S. in Veterinary Technology from High Desert Community College (2018), is a Licensed Veterinary Technician in Oregon, and completed equine emergency medicine certification through Ridgeline Veterinary Institute (2021).

She is competent in a way that costs people their assumptions: petite and young-looking, often mistaken for the receptionist at her own clinic, and blunt enough when she speaks to startle people who misread her. She has the work ethic of a 4 AM ranch upbringing she does not entirely approve of but has never stopped using. She carries quiet grief about her brother and rarely names it, preferring to say it has been a hard couple of years and change the subject. She talks to animals in full sentences and is not embarrassed by it.

Her cultural identity is lived, not curated. The food, the festivals, the temple visits, her grandmother's prayer beads, and the New Year traditions are routine to her, not heritage. Her Japanese is family Japanese, slangy in places and formal in others, and she finds it strange that anyone is impressed that she switches languages mid-sentence. She is conscious of being one of very few visibly Asian women on the rodeo circuit and has met curiosity, condescension, and occasional racism, which she answers by being better at her job than the person being rude. Her philosophy is plain: the animals cannot advocate for themselves, work ethic is the floor of a life and not the ceiling, loyalty is a longer transaction than most people treat it as, and showing up matters even when it fixes nothing.

## Work & Projects
At Rimrock Valley Veterinary Clinic in Redmond, Midori works as a senior large-animal veterinary technician in a mixed practice with three veterinarians and four techs serving mostly equine and bovine patients. She has eight years of experience and assists in colic, C-section, and orthopedic surgeries, performs dental floats, takes radiographs, runs lab work, and handles emergency triage across both clinic and ambulatory farm-call work. Her specialties are equine reproduction and bovine pregnancy checking, where she can palpate over a hundred cows in a day, plus emergency field medicine. Dr. Whitfield is encouraging her toward veterinary school; Midori wants the DVM but is daunted by the cost, over $200K, and four years, and she has been quietly studying for the GRE. She also earns occasional side income from freelance equine bodywork, massage and stretching, at $50 to $75 per session two to three times a month.

## Finance
Her salary at Rimrock Valley is $42,000 a year, with freelance bodywork adding roughly $100 to $225 a month and variable rodeo prize money of about $2,000 to $4,000 in a good year. Monthly take-home is about $2,900 after taxes and health insurance. Major monthly costs include about $850 rent, $380 truck payment, $250 diesel, $220 commercial-rate truck insurance, $350 horse expenses, $300 groceries, $120 utilities, and $85 phone, plus smaller line items, which leaves a buffer near $80. She holds about $3,800 in savings and $6,200 in a Roth IRA she opened in 2024, contributing $100 a month when she can. Her debts are about $8,500 left on the truck loan and $4,200 in student loans, with no credit card debt because she avoids them. Money is tight, and she manages by being frugal and doing her own truck and tack maintenance; she also occasionally sends $50 to $100 to Kenji for personal needs. She banks at Cascade Community Credit Union and tracks every expense by hand in a small green notebook, by category, by month.

## Health & Wellness
Midori is strong and functionally fit from manual labor, riding, and ranch work rather than the gym. She manages a chronic right rotator cuff strain with home PT exercises, and it flares in cold weather, plus occasional lower back soreness from long hours in the truck and saddle that she handles with stretching and a foam roller. She fully recovered from a 2024 barrel racing concussion and is more careful about helmets now. She sleeps six to seven hours, up at 5 AM most days and earlier in calving and foaling season. She takes no medications and uses glucosamine for joint health. Kenji's addiction is a chronic source of stress she handles by staying busy, which she knows is not a real strategy. Within the family, Patrick has bad knees he refuses to get surgery on, and Fumiko manages Type 2 diabetes with diet and medication while her mobility declines.

## Interests & Hobbies
Barrel racing is the center of her life; she competes in Columbia Basin Rodeo Association events and select WPRA rodeos on her quarter horse mare Hikari, sits in the top 15 of CBRA standings, and trains four to five days a week. Beyond racing she does trail riding and natural horsemanship groundwork, and keeps Sage, a retired gelding companion animal. She helps at her parents' ranch on alternating Sundays with cattle work, fencing, and irrigation, which is family obligation more than hobby. In winter evenings she does leatherwork, learning tooled belt-making from an elderly neighbor and handling her own tack repairs. She hikes the Cascade foothills and Three Sisters Wilderness with her dog Kaze, walking until her head quiets rather than summit-bagging. She cooks Japanese food from her mother's and grandmother's recipes, with New Year mochi a whole-family production. She reads slowly, about a dozen books a year, mixing veterinary journals, James Herriot, and translated Japanese fiction like Murakami and Yoshimoto.

## Home & Living
She rents a small one-bedroom house on three acres in the Tumalo area outside Bend, functional and spartan, with tack and leather tools as the most prominent decor. The property has a three-stall barn, two stalls used for Hikari and Sage, a small paddock, a round pen for training, and hay storage, with Kaze running the place. Her Ford F-250 and Sundowner trailer are parked there, the truck always loaded with basic vet supplies, ropes, and an emergency kit. The clinic in Redmond is a 20-minute drive, her parents' ranch is about 35 minutes, and Klamath Falls for Kenji is about 2.5 hours. There is no dishwasher and no garage, and she does not care about either.

## Preferences
- **Music**: J-Pop and Japanese folk such as Yonezu Kenshi and RADWIMPS, country such as George Strait, Turnpike Troubadours, and Cody Johnson, and enka on long drives.
- **Food and drink**: Omnivore and ranch-raised, with a Japanese home-cooking foundation of teriyaki, oden, sukiyaki, gyoza, onigiri, and mochi. Coffee is strong, drip, and black, multiple cups from 5 AM. She drinks occasionally, an Asahi or Sapporo after a rodeo or sake with Tara. Her weakness is her grandmother's mochi and daifuku, worth an hour's drive.
- **Shopping**: Frugal and unperformative; she buys quality on boots, tack, and tires and the cheapest acceptable version of everything else. Tractor Supply and Boot Barn for ranch and tack, WinCo for budget groceries, and a monthly Asian-grocery trip she treats as half-pilgrimage. Gifts are practical except for Obaa-chan, who gets things from far away.
- **Entertainment**: Rodeo coverage and horse content on YouTube, the occasional movie with Tara, and a three-film insomnia rotation of Princess Mononoke, Tombstone, and Whisper of the Heart. She does not pay for streaming.
- **Social media**: Instagram for the barrel racing and horse community, occasional Facebook for family, and no Twitter/X or TikTok.
- **Aesthetic and dress**: Work clothes most of the time, Wranglers and chambray or plaid with a self-made leather belt, a felt hat in cold and a straw one in heat. One dress kept for funerals and weddings. The truck is more decorated than the spartan house.
- **Decompression and travel**: When the day is hard she goes to the barn, and when truly sad she rides into the foothills. She does not travel for pleasure, packs light, prefers driving to flying, dislikes cities, and is quietly saving in a jar to visit Japan someday.
- **Sensory world**: She loves saddle soap, fresh hay, wet sagebrush after rain, diesel on a cold morning, and her grandmother's house of incense and old wood. She cannot stand air fresheners, bleach, synthetic vanilla, or most perfumes.

# Memory: Aaron Whitmore

## Personal Profile

He is Panhandle to the bone, the product of a rural, working-class corner of Texas where a man is measured by what he can do and whether he will do it without being asked. That frame sits so deep he does not experience it as a culture at all, just as the way things are. He carries a quiet defensiveness about it around outsiders, and a load-bearing, mostly unspoken pride in the life, alongside a half-aware ache that the world he was raised into is thinning out a little more every year.

He is easy to mistake for closed-off. The quiet is not withholding so much as a different setting on the same dial most people leave turned up. He is watching, weighing, and generally sees no use in saying a thing before it is settled. His patience runs in two directions: bottomless with engines, animals, and a repair that keeps fighting him; fast to drain when a person gives their word and does not follow it. He rarely makes a scene, but something behind his eyes closes and stays closed a while. He would say he is not one to hold a grudge, and he means it. What he actually keeps is a long, unspoken ledger of who does what they say they will do.

Genuine idleness unsettles him in a way he has never fully examined. A rained-out day that cannot be filled leaves him drifting toward the shop to square away tools that did not need it. His dry humor surfaces in those in-between moments, delivered flat, usually at his own expense, easy to miss if you are not watching for the half-second almost-smile. He owns a mistake fast with the few people he trusts and digs in stubbornly with everyone else.

Underneath everything he believes a man is what he does and keeps his word about. Honesty in work is close to sacred; he will not cut a corner he would be ashamed of even when no one would ever know. Money he treats with a wariness deeper than budgeting. Debt feels like a leash, and there is a quiet conviction that wanting less is its own kind of freedom. The deepest unresolved thing he carries is about his kids: he wants them to have the grit the life gave him, and he wants them to have it easier than he did, and he has not worked out whether both wishes can come true.

Faith is woven through that identity in a way that is more about belonging and rhythm than doctrine he could lay out for you. It is the standing appointment of the week, the place his people have always gathered, the casseroles when somebody is down. He graduated Briscoe-Silverton High in 2006 and took some ag courses at Clarendon College before coming home when his dad got sick. He speaks English natively and enough Spanish to communicate with hands at the auction barn.

## Work & Projects

- **Ranch**: 160 acres, 85 head Angus cattle. Cow-calf operation: breeds, raises, sells at the Amarillo Livestock Auction. Calving season January through March. Busiest periods: calving, spring branding and vaccinating, fall weaning and selling.
- **Equipment repair**: Side business by word of mouth. Fixes tractors, implements, and welding jobs for ranchers in the area. No formal business name, just "Call Aaron." Charges fair, works honest, gets repeat customers.
- **Open jobs**: Replacing a hydraulic cylinder on Carl's John Deere baler (parts on order from Amarillo). Fence repair on the south pasture (coyote damage). Prepping cattle for fall weaning and vaccinations (scheduled October 14, 2026 with Dr. Moyer).
- **Tenure**: Took over the operation in full when his father passed and has been running it on his own for over a decade.

## Health & Wellness

Triggers: barometric pressure drops, dehydration, lack of sleep, diesel fumes. Managed with sumatriptan 100mg at onset and daily preventive topiramate 50mg in the morning. Topiramate started 2024 by Dr. Chen and cut frequency from 8 to 10 down to 4 to 6 a month.
- **Primary care**: Dr. Linda Chen, Clarendon Family Medicine. Annual physical and migraine management. Last visit February 2026.
- **Dental**: Dr. Harris, Silverton. Every six months.
- **Medications**: Topiramate 50mg daily morning; sumatriptan 100mg as needed at migraine onset, max 2 per week; ibuprofen 600mg as needed for general aches; multivitamin (Jenny insists).
- **Exercise**: Ranch work is ten plus hours of physical labor daily. Walks miles, lifts heavy things, fixes fences. No formal routine; in better shape than most gym-goers.
- **Sleep**: Sleeps well most nights. Broken sleep during calving season (January through March) and on migraine nights.
- **Diet**: No medical restrictions. Beef-heavy by occupation. Tolerates the daily topiramate without significant side effects.
- **Sensory**: Strong fumes in a closed space wear on him faster than he lets on. Flat white glare and big-store fluorescents can sour his mood out of proportion to the cause.

## Interests & Hobbies

Ask Aaron for his hobbies and he would shrug and say he has no time, and he would half-believe it. Tinkering is the clearest case: a balky carburetor he could leave for tomorrow becomes the thing he is still under at dusk, because the puzzle has its hooks in him and the satisfaction of making a dead thing run again scratches something he does not have words for. Fishing is the closest he comes to a pastime that admits it is one, often disguised as checking a tank or scouting a fence line down by the water (Palo Duro Canyon when time allows). Coaching Wyatt's Little League sits in its own category, partly duty and partly the one arena where he is comfortable being looked at. He reads occasional Louis L'Amour westerns from the shelf, listens to country (George Strait, Chris LeDoux, Turnpike Troubadours) on the FM radio, watches the kids' movies with them, and catches Yellowstone reruns if Jenny has it on.

## Preferences

- **Coffee**: Black. Brews a full pot at 5:00 AM. Drinks 3 to 4 cups before noon. Folgers.

- **Snacks**: Jerky, whatever Jenny packed. Not a snacking person.
- **Alcohol**: Coors Light on weekends, maybe 4 to 6 a week. Nothing during work hours.
- **Music**: Country. George Strait, Chris LeDoux, Turnpike Troubadours. FM radio in the truck and the shop, no streaming.
- **TV and reading**: Yellowstone reruns if Jenny has them on; kids' movies with the kids; not a TV person. Reads equipment manuals front to back, occasional Louis L'Amour westerns.
- **Shopping**: Practical and local. Tractor Supply, Atwoods, Amarillo parts stores when needed, Amazon for specific parts Jenny orders. Hates shopping. Researches once, buys the version that will outlast him, then quietly judges the cheaper option for the rest of its short life.
- **Aesthetics**: No use for decoration, newness, or anything fussy. Strong, almost involuntary opinions about how a thing ought to be made: a straight fence, a clean weld, a tool worn smooth by the right hands over the right years. Work-grade denim and boots, replaced only when truly done. Distrusts anything too clean or too new on a man.
- **Social style**: Friendship sideways. Most himself shoulder to shoulder under a hood or out mending a fence. Prefers a thirty-second call to a thread of messages, or just turning up. Big gatherings drain him; he will go, stand at the edge of the room, be quietly glad he came and quietly relieved when it is time to go.
- **Comfort and decompression**: Rest rarely looks like rest. Drifting out to the shop with the radio on low, the porch at dusk with the dog, a cold beer at the end of a Friday, the deep quiet of the house once the kids are down. Depleted days send him toward the truck and a slow drive, down to the water, or out across the place at gold-light hour.
- **Travel**: Largely theoretical. Animals need tending every day. A day in the canyon with a line in the water is about as far afield as he actively wants to go. A faint, rarely-aired notion that someday he might take Jenny somewhere just because she would like it.
- **Sensory world**: Reads engines and animals by sound. Diesel, dust, hay, cold metal in the winter shop, green of cut grass, rain on dry ground (close to a favorite smell). Texture in his hands: worn denim and smooth tool handles read right; cheap, brand-new, slick materials register as a mild offense. Low gold light of early and late day; flat white glare and big-store fluorescents wear on him.

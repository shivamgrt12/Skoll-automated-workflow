# Pony Cash-Fit Analysis

## The answer

**Open.** The cash room is plainly there, but the local starter-pony market is thin enough on actual sale evidence that committing without seeing a specific animal at a specific price would be premature. What would close this to a yes: a sound 8-to-12-year-old broke pony seen in person at the stockhorse show in Amarillo the first weekend of November, or at the Donley County 4H sale and tack swap in Clarendon late October, for an honest price under $2,500 all-in.

## The cash math behind the answer

Post-sale cash position, honest case: **$72,320**, with the $25,000 equipment emergency fund target satisfied and **$47,320 of working room** above it.

A sound starter pony from regional chatter sits in the **$1,500 to $2,500** range.

First-year setup costs around the place: tack $300 to $500, small shelter improvement off the existing barn $200 to $400, vet and farrier first round $200, extra feed for one pony November through March at about $30 a month, $150.

**All-in first-year: $2,500 to $3,800.**

Against $47,320 of working room above the equipment fund target, the pony fits without crowding the fund or touching the card. On cash alone, this is comfortably yes.

## Why the conclusion is open and not yes

The price evidence is thin in two ways.

First, the listings on small-acreage horse properties around the Panhandle do not move on pony pricing. They tell Aaron about land, not animals. That surface is a weak signal here.

Second, the regional ranching chatter confirms that the actual local pony market runs word-of-mouth, not on the kind of forum or listing thread that produces a hard price. A range of $1,500 to $2,500 is the consensus, but the variance inside that range is wide enough that the right answer depends on the specific pony.

The right move: walk through the stockhorse show first weekend of November, look at what is actually being moved, talk to a couple of the sellers. The Donley County 4H tack swap late October is the other place to check, free admission, real local sellers. If a sound broke pony shows up at an honest price, the cash is there and the conclusion closes to yes. If not, the conclusion stays open and Emma's birthday gets a different pony plan from Jenny, no card touched.

## What this analysis does not do

It does not stage a card purchase or any other purchase. It does not message Jenny about the pony math. The prompt is explicit that conversation is on hold until Aaron decides. The figure here stays inside the house and is not surfaced in any artifact that leaves the kitchen table.

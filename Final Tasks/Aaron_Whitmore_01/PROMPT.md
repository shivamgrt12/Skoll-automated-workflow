The fall sale is the only paycheck that matters this year and I need to walk into Amarillo with the right calves at the right weight and the right floor in my head, so take this off my plate and get me to one honest read on what the year actually pays, what walks to the truck on the October 14 weaning day, and what stays back. Go through the books for the whole year so far and tell me where we really stand, not where Jenny and I think we stand at the kitchen table, every bill and every receipt and every deposit lined up against the bank reality and the year-to-date true cost of running the place, vet and feed and fuel and parts and the rest, and when two sources disagree the newest and most authoritative wins and I want the source you trusted named and the one you set aside named too. I am not chasing a round number from something I told you back in the spring. Walk the herd one head at a time against what Dr. Moyer actually has on paper for vaccinations and pregnancy checks and what shows up in the books for purchases and sales since calving, settle the count for real, then build the saleable calf class off that, the heifers I am holding back, the cows on the cull list because of age or open status or anything Moyer has flagged, and tell me what is genuinely going to the auction barn against what stays. Off that population, build the sale picture one step at a time the way I would on paper, the expected weaning weight on this year's calf crop, the going Panhandle price per pound by class as best you can read it from the auction barn newsletters and what the regional ranching chatter is saying about the last three or four sale days, net of the auction commission and the hauling, and lay out the dollar range from conservative to honest to optimistic so I know what floor I would walk back from. While that is running, comb the equipment side. Pull the Panhandle weather for the run up to October 14 and the auction window after it, flag any pressure drop that is going to put me on my back with a migraine because I cannot lose a day inside that stretch, and read the pasture against the satellite drought maps so I know what the grass can actually carry through winter and whether I am hauling more hay than the budget thinks. Then sit Emma's pony against the real cash picture. Jenny has been talking about a Shetland or a small Quarter for her eighth birthday and I have been turning it over in my head, but I am not touching the equipment emergency fund for it and I will not put it on a card, so do the math against where the books actually land after the sale and the rest of the fourth-quarter run of bills and tell me honest whether it fits without crowding the fund, and if the evidence is thin on what a sound starter pony actually goes for around here keep the conclusion open rather than forcing me a yes or no I will resent come spring. Fit the whole stretch around the dates I cannot move, Dr. Harris on November 6, Emma's birthday on November 14, Thanksgiving on the 26th, Dr. Chen on December 10, Bobby home on Christmas, and the standing midweek practice and the weekly service. Hand me back a sale-and-weaning brief I can act off, the cash picture for the year that I can argue with line by line, and a clean read on the repair book so I know what to hit between the next stretch and the day, the kind of brief that names cold what is solid enough for me to lean on and what is still too thin, where the gaps are, and who I owe a call to. Do not order the cylinder or any other part on my behalf, do not message Carl or the repair customers, do not say a thing to Jenny about the pony math yet, draft what has to be drafted and hold for me to sign off, and keep the financials inside the house regardless of who asks.

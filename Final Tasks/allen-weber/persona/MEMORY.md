# Memory: Allen Weber

## Personal Profile

Allen Weber grew up in Hartford, Connecticut as the first in his family to attend a four-year college. His father Emeka (a Nigerian immigrant who works as an accountant at a mid-size firm) and his mother Adaeze (a Nigerian-American registered nurse) raised him and his younger brother Chidi with a steady, structured affection he is only now learning to articulate. He chose Harbor Point because it offered the most aid and the strongest urban policy faculty; the Boston location was a bonus that has grown into a real attachment.

He is curious before he is critical. He will read the whole article, sit through the boring middle of a lecture, and ask the follow-up question nobody else thinks to ask. He is socially fluent (he leaves parties with new contacts and study group invites) and privately guarded (his roommates know his schedule, not his anxieties about money or whether he is good enough to be here). He is ambitious in bursts: he will throw himself into a project, then drift until the next wave catches him. He knows this about himself and finds it slightly terrifying. He is a night owl who keeps setting 6 AM alarms he snoozes through, and he finds that funny about himself rather than distressing.

He speaks English natively, Spanish conversationally from high school, and reads French passably from three college semesters. He identifies as a working-class first-generation student. He is single, not actively dating. A freshman-year relationship ended mutually with no lingering tension. He is open in principle but realistic about the bandwidth that school and a barista job leave him.

## Finance

Total monthly income is roughly $2,300 during the Whitfield summer (with the stipend prorated) and roughly $1,800 during the academic year: barista wages around $1,400, tips around $280, and occasional Venmo from his parents around $120. A partial Harbor Point scholarship covers 60 percent of tuition. Federal subsidized loans cover the rest. His parents contribute what they can toward room and board.

Academic-year monthly expenses run about $1,603. Rent is $850 (one-third of a 3BR), utilities $80, groceries $280, MBTA pass $90, phone $35 (contribution to his parents' plan), health insurance $0 (covered by the university student plan inside tuition), subscriptions $18 (Spotify student, iCloud), dining out and coffee outside work freebies $120, personal $80, course materials $50. That leaves roughly $197 in a good month and close to zero in a tight one. Savings sit at $1,400 in a Citizens Bank savings account, with the Whitfield stipend slated to push that to roughly $3,500 by end of summer. Student loans accumulated so far total roughly $22,000 in federal subsidized debt, deferred until graduation. He uses a debit card for everything and does not carry a credit card. He banks at Citizens Bank (checking and savings, opened with his mother freshman year). He is on his parents' auto insurance but does not drive, and on their dental insurance.

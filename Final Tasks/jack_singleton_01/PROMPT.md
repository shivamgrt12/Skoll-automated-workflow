--- TURN 1 ---
I want to walk into the new season with the boat right, the money honest, and the charter book straight, so I need you to take everything I hand you and give me the real version, not the one I carry around in my head off the water. I have been running fuel, bait, parts, the slip, and the engine note through the books all season and the picture has gotten muddy, so pull the whole run of what has gone out and what has come in and tell me where I actually stand heading into the tight stretch before the crabs pay again. I punch a lot of these in late and tired, so go through them careful and tell me what you truly find rather than taking every line at face value. Give me the honest monthly nut, what the fixed bills pull against what is sitting in savings, and tell me straight how many weeks of cushion that leaves before the first real haul lands. Show me the math on it, do not just hand me a number.

Then there are the pots. Every one has to be tagged and fit to fish before the inspection, and my own count of what is rigged, what is stored, and what needs rebuilt has never once matched what I think I have. Reconcile my register against what the state has me down for and tell me which figure I should actually be trusting before I spend a dime, flag the strings that are short or beat up, and build me the loadout that puts a full working deck of good pots out for the first trip with the rest sorted before the inspector comes. I do not want a wrong count driving the order, so run it down and be sure of it.

Once you know what I am short, price it out. Wire, frames, zincs, culling rings, bait for the first week, fuel for the first block of trips, and lay it out trip by trip so I can see the cash going out before a dime comes in. Draft the orders so they are ready and waiting on my signature, but do not place anything, because I want my own eyes on anything past a small bill before it ever goes out the door.

The crew comes back three strong and they do not all pull the same day rate, so keep each man's number straight to what he is actually owed and let nothing but the real rate set his pay. I will handle the pay lines myself, so just bring me the clean figures for each man and leave it there.

Then the charter book. My agent is filling the summer slate and I need it clean before he sells another day. Go through the repeat clients, line up the days already spoken for, and price the season off what a full day and a half day truly run for me now, because I do not want an old figure setting the price on new water. Give me the honest read on what the summer clears once the agent and the deposits already down are accounted for. Watch the deposits close so nothing gets counted where it does not belong. Draft the confirmations for the clients whose days are locked, but do not send them, I will read every one before it leaves my hand.

Keep an eye on the forecast and the tides while you plan those first trips, because there is no sense loading a full deck of pots into a blow. Pull the paperwork that has to be current before the boat leaves the slip, the tagging forms, the liability waivers, the haul out and the insurance, and tell me plainly what is signed and what is still open so I can chase down whatever is missing before the inspector shows.

Anything that touches the boat money stays between me and the bookkeeper, and if you cannot get me a clean number because a source is thin or two of them fight and neither one wins, say so and hold it open instead of handing me a tidy guess. I would rather know what you are not sure of than get a neat answer I cannot trust, so where the sources disagree, lay out what each one says and tell me which you would lean on and why, and where they simply run out, tell me that too.

When it is done I want a straight read on where the money sits and whether the season opens with enough behind me, with the math shown and not just the answer, a readiness sheet that ties the pots, the crew, and the paperwork into one picture so I know the boat is right for opening day and the inspection after, and the charter book priced clean with the confirmations drafted and waiting on my word. Write it up plain, in numbers I can read at the kitchen table, and bring it back to me, and I will be the one to pull the triggers.

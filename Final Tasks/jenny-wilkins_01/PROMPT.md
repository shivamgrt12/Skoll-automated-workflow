--- TURN 1 ---
The whole late stretch of the year has quietly gotten away from me and I want it trued up into one plan I can actually lean on, because the family weekend in the middle of December is the thing I care most about getting right and everything before it feeds into whether I arrive at that door tired or ready. Claire and Thomas and the two grandchildren are coming to us for the weekend and I want the house, the food, the gifts, and the calendar around it all to be settled well ahead rather than scrambled the week of. Start with the days themselves, because I have a bad feeling the schedule no longer agrees with itself. Between the specialists in Hartford, the dental and eye visits closer to home, the pharmacy runs, the garden club obligations, and the family drive to Stamford for the earlier holiday, I have accepted so many confirmations and nudged so many times over the months that I have honestly lost track of which version of each date is the real one, and a few of the entries feel like they are quietly out of step with the notes that came in later. I need every appointment and commitment from the plant swap through the family weekend walked down against whatever the most recent word on it actually was, the genuine collisions surfaced, and anything that cannot be pinned down flagged plainly rather than smoothed over, because I would rather know a thing is uncertain than be told a tidy answer that turns out wrong when Harriet and I are already in the car. Once the spine of the days is trustworthy I want the gifts and the reading sorted, since half the joy of that weekend is what gets handed across the table. Oliver and I have the standing book swap and I never want to hand him something below or above where he actually is as a reader, Bea has just turned sixteen and deserves something chosen for her and not for the occasion, and there are titles I have been meaning to bring into the club and the personal log that I want confirmed as real editions I can actually get hold of before I promise anyone anything, so please run those down properly and tell me where a copy genuinely lives rather than assuming the first listing that looks right is the one. Then the table, which is the part I fuss over most. The little ones asked for my butternut squash soup again and I want to make it for the six of us without setting off the reflux that has made a quiet enemy of half the ingredients I used to reach for, so I need the recipe scaled honestly to the headcount, the things I can no longer eat swapped for something that still tastes like the dish and not like an apology, and the provisioning worked out so the shortfall from the pantry comes in as a sensible order from the usual grocery without tipping past what I would sign off on without a second thought. While the food is being thought through, the garden cannot be left to fend for itself, because the cold has already turned on me for the season and there is real work owed before the ground closes. I keep a long record of what is planted where and what has lived or sulked or died each spring, and I want that record actually reconciled against the divisions I promised to bring to the swap and the winter tasks still hanging open, so what I carry to share is drawn from clumps that are genuinely ready and the beds that matter most go into the cold well protected. That notebook has been edited by my own hand for forty years and I have caught it contradicting itself more than once lately, so where a claim about what survived or what was moved looks shaky I want it run down properly and settled before I act on it rather than taken on faith, and I want to know plainly what you leaned on and what you put aside. Pull all of this together into a single ordered picture of the weeks into that weekend that I can read start to finish and trust, with the medication timing and the cold-weather cautions kept in view the way they have to be, and a clear-eyed account of the food and what still needs buying so nothing is discovered missing the morning of. A few softer notes to hold while you work. Anything that needs to go out to Michael or Ruth or the garden folk, to the family, or to any of the offices should be written and set ready for me to look over and nothing sent or booked on its own, since I like to read a thing twice before it leaves my hands. Keep the private parts private and shaped to who is actually being spoken to, the way you always do, and do not go reaching into the bank or the insurance side of things for me, that is Harriet's country and not yours to wander into. And where the evidence for something is just thin, leave it honestly open and say so rather than reaching for a confident answer, because I have spent a working life learning that certainty which has stopped looking is not certainty worth having.

About

A reference desk for one quiet life

Thirty-two years behind a reference desk taught me that most questions have an answer somewhere, and the trick is knowing which shelf to look on. That instinct runs through everything now: the spice rack is alphabetized, the seed packets are sorted by sowing month, and the bookshelves follow a private system four decades old.

I live in Glastonbury, Connecticut, in a Cape Cod house with yellow kitchen walls and copper pots, alongside my spouse Harriet. The days run on a gentle rhythm of coffee at the window, a morning walk, the garden in season, and a book always within reach.

The Garden

A quarter acre, edited for nearly forty years

I keep the perennial borders and the shade garden, and Harriet keeps the vegetable beds. The rule has not changed in all that time: move what is unhappy, divide what is crowded, and resist the urge to add a third of anything.

Shade border

Hostas by the dozen, native ferns, and the silver glow of Brunnera in the dim corner.

Sunny border

Echinacea in three colors and a naturalized colony of black-eyed Susans the finches adore.

The greenhouse

Snapdragons and foxglove under lights in deep winter, which is an act of faith and exactly that.

The Reading Life

Read for the sentences as much as the plot

Three or four books a month, across paper, audio, and the reading app, with English mysteries read before American ones. A forty-year commonplace notebook holds the passages worth keeping. Below is where things stand at the moment.

On the nightstand

Quiet literary fiction for the dark evenings, with a nature title kept by the chair for shorter sittings.

Next at the table

Lighter and shorter by group request after a heavy winter pick. I am saving a long-loved novel of friendship and marriage to lead in the fall.

The reliable shelf

Mary Oliver, Wendell Berry, the Aubrey-Maturin novels, and a few Agatha Christies for when the day needs softening.

Volunteering

Part habit, part identity

I help with the library's quarterly used-book sale, sit with the garden club, and give the occasional guest lecture to library science students on the craft of the reference interview. The work keeps a hand in the thing I did for a living, which turns out to be the point.

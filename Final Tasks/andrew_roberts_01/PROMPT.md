--- TURN 1 ---

It's the first of the month. The coffee's poured and the radio's down low, so let's get the finance walk done before the day starts making its demands.

I'll tell you why I'm doing this properly this time instead of the usual quick glance over the balances. I carry a rough household budget around in my head, and I've carried it so long that I've mostly stopped questioning it. Lately I've had a nagging feeling that the tidy figures up here and what my accounts are actually doing quietly stopped agreeing with each other a good while back, and I let it slide. Forty years of pulling failed machines apart taught me one thing above all the rest. The number you never measure is usually the number that's gone wrong on you. So I'd sooner face the truth of it now than keep flattering myself for another year.

Here's the work. Take my accounts apart and put them back together honestly. Walk everything that actually cleared, the checking and the card both, and rebuild what each ordinary part of my life genuinely costs me now, the steady bills, the irregular ones, and all the small stuff that quietly adds up while I'm not paying it any attention. Trace every line the way I used to trace a load path through a frame before I trusted my weight to it, not a quick skim across the top. I want the numbers that are true today, and I want to be able to stand behind every one of them when you're done. I'm not after a tidy average or a round figure that feels about right in the moment. I want each part rebuilt from what the money actually did, so that the total ends up something measured rather than something I only hoped was so.

Then set that honest cost against my income. Where what I remember and what the record shows part ways, name the gap plainly and in real dollars. Don't round it off soft to spare my feelings. If I've been telling myself a comfortable little story about what I spend somewhere, I'd far rather have it caught than coddled. Once that's settled, do the arithmetic slowly and carefully and tell me what's genuinely left to me each month after everything real is covered. That leftover is the figure the rest of my decisions all lean on, so I want it right, and I want to see plainly how you arrived at it.

When the household side is straight, widen the lens. When one of the kids asks how the old man is really doing, and they do ask, more gently than they think I notice, I'd like one honest answer sitting in a single place, not me fumbling half of it out from memory over the phone. So pull the rest of where my money actually sits into the same picture, right alongside the bank and the card, and total the whole of it into something I can say out loud without wincing. While you're in among the signed paperwork, note plainly what protections I've already got in place, just a plain list of what exists, so the picture shows both what I have and what's looking after it. Gather all of that into one clean, corrected write-up and set it down somewhere durable in my own notes, so it's sitting there ready the day one of them does ask and I can hand it over without any fuss or scrambling. I'd hate for the kids to have to sit down someday and piece all of this together cold, so I'd sooner leave it plain and current now, while I'm still the one who knows where every last thing of mine is kept.

The other half of this is for Phil. He prepares my return every year as a family favor and has never once grumbled about it, so the least I can do in return is hand him something tidy to work from instead of a mess to untangle. Pull together my charitable giving and the medical odds-and-ends from across the year into an organized summary he can file straight from. This is the part where I want real care taken. My own record-keeping is honestly a bit of a shoebox, and I'd much rather give Phil a clean summary I can stand behind than a padded one that looks generous and falls apart the moment he leans any weight on it. Draft it up for him and then leave it sitting with me to read over first.

That's the shape of the whole thing. Nothing fancy about it, just the real picture, honest and mine to keep, with Phil's piece drafted and waiting on my word. Take whatever time it needs to come out right. I've watched a good many capable people paper over a gap with a confident-looking number, and I have never once, in forty years, trusted the number.

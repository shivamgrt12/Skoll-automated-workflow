# Memory: Ayumi Meade

## Personal Profile

Ayumi Haruko "Yumi" Meade is Japanese-American on her mother's side and Irish-American on her father's. She holds a B.A. in Education from Thornfield State University, an M.Ed. in Educational Leadership from Cedarbrook University, and an Ed.D. in Education Policy from Ridgemont University.

She carries a composure that people mistake for calm while her mind runs scenario analyses underneath. She thinks in systems, always hunting the upstream cause, which makes her exceptional at her job and occasionally hard to live with; Brendan's phrase for it is "You're optimizing dinner again." Her patience for institutional process is deep but ends abruptly, and she is slightly embarrassed by the pattern. Her perfectionism is managed rather than eliminated; she holds herself to standards she would never impose on others. She is more emotionally transparent than she believes; Angela can read her mood from the speed of her walk down the hallway. Her laugh is bigger than her professional reputation suggests and fills rooms.

Culturally, she experiences her two inheritances as overlapping territories rather than halves. The Japanese side runs through food, language fragments, aesthetics, and the bone-deep rule that you show up for family without conditions. The Irish-American side carries tolerance for loud argument and comfort with directness. She thinks in English, cooks in Japanese, and swears in both; her Japanese is domestic and familial, not business grade. She is the first Asian-American superintendent in her district and carries that label with obligation more than pride; she does the representation work because someone has to, but resents the energy it costs.

Her deepest conviction is that public institutions are the scaffolding of a just society and that the people inside them deserve more respect than the culture provides. She believes in earned authority over positional authority, uses her title sparingly, and judges leaders who lead from the letterhead. Her ambition has shifted from being the person who solves the problem to building the team that solves it, though the shift is incomplete. Family is less a stated value than a condition she inhabits, and her one unappeasable fear is that the job erodes her presence in her children's lives.

Socially she leads by listening first and speaking last, a habit that is partly cultural, partly tactical, and partly scar tissue from being talked over early in her career. She keeps work and personal relationships distinct, is loud and willing to be wrong out loud only at the family table, needs a role to enjoy large social events, and is slow to make new friends out of scarcity of time rather than unfriendliness.

## Key Relationships

- **Brendan Meade** (husband, 52): General contractor at Meade & Sons Construction, the family business with his brother. Steady and patient, handles the home front when her schedule explodes. Married 24 years and they still make each other laugh; they argue affectionately about grill technique.
- **Hana Meade** (daughter, 19): Sophomore at Ashvale State University studying pre-law, ambitious and outspoken about identity politics. Yumi sees herself in Hana and finds it both thrilling and terrifying. Calls home regularly just to talk.
- **Kenji Meade** (son, 16): Junior at Cedar Bluff High School, inside Yumi's own district. Plays varsity baseball, easygoing and social, moves through the world with little friction. Yumi works hard not to be "the superintendent" at his school events.
- **Mika Meade** (daughter, 12): Seventh grader at Willow Creek Middle School. Creative and sensitive; writes poetry that is better than a twelve-year-old's has any right to be and performs in school theater. Yumi worries Mika feels overlooked. A coming-of-age celebration is being planned for her, with Portland reception halls compared; Yumi wants it special but not extravagant, reflecting family values over spectacle.
- **Sachiko Tanaka, "Bāchan"** (grandmother, 88): Retired home health aide, widowed, lives four blocks away. The anchor of the entire extended family; her house is where the whole clan gathers and her handwritten recipe book is Yumi's culinary inheritance. Yumi's emotional touchstone and backup childcare.
- **Takeshi Tanaka** (brother, 47): Fire captain with the Portland Fire Department. Protective older-brother energy despite being younger. Lives in Beaverton with his family. Yumi's chief hanafuda rival.
- **Naomi Ishikawa** (best friend, 49): Principal at Evergreen Elementary, in Yumi's district. Friends since college; the only person who can tell Yumi she is wrong and be heard.
- **Patricia Odom** (school board president, 58): Retired corporate executive. Supports Yumi's vision but pushes hard on fiscal discipline; respectful but tense during budget season.
- **David Kowalski** (teachers' union president, 45): High school science teacher, passionate and confrontational. Their dynamic mixes genuine disagreement with mutual respect underneath.
- **Dr. Grace Whitmore** (deputy superintendent, 53): Yumi's most trusted administrator. Calm and detail oriented; runs day-to-day operations when Yumi is at external meetings.
- **Angela Marsh** (executive assistant, 38): Three years with Yumi. Manages calendar and communications with surgical precision.
- **Dr. Linda Hayashi** (primary care physician): Practices at Riverview Family Health Center and manages Yumi's diabetes care.

## Work & Projects

- Superintendent of Cascade Ridge Unified School District: 22,000 students, 31 schools, 2,800 employees, serving a diverse and working-class community.
- Fourth year in the role; contract runs through June 2028.
- Facing a $4.8 million budget shortfall for the 2026 to 2027 fiscal year, driven by declining state funding and rising operational costs. The board is split 3-2 on solutions. Yumi is proposing administrative consolidation plus a facilities bond rather than teacher layoffs.
- Overseeing the flagship Mirai STEM initiative at two east-side schools; a state innovation grant application is in progress, and she sees the grant as proof the east-side investment can outlast the current funding cycle.
- Navigating tense negotiations with the teachers' union over proposed bell schedule changes to accommodate expanded tutoring hours.
- A district equity audit released in January 2026 identified resource disparities between west-side and east-side schools; she is addressing it publicly and it is politically sensitive.
- Active in the Portland Asian Pacific Education Alliance. She has been approached about running for the Oregon State Board of Education in 2028 and is considering it, though the timing is terrible.

## Health & Wellness

- Type 2 diabetes, diagnosed three years ago. Managed with metformin 1000mg twice daily and dietary changes with mixed adherence.
- Care managed by Dr. Linda Hayashi at Riverview Family Health Center, with regular A1C monitoring.
- Working on more vegetables and less sodium at her doctor's urging; compliance is inconsistent.
- The early morning waterfront walk is the load-bearing wall of her wellbeing; on days it gets skipped she feels the deficit by noon.
- Carries stress in her neck and jaw, grinds her teeth at night, and wears a night guard.
- Gets tension headaches during board meeting weeks; keeps ibuprofen in her desk and car.
- Annual diabetic retinal screening; last results were clear.
- Sleeps 5.5 to 6 hours most nights and cannot turn her brain off.
- Physical stillness is harder for her than activity; she fidgets in long meetings and has never made it past a third meditation session.

## Interests & Hobbies

- Cooks elaborate Japanese dishes from Bāchan's handwritten recipe book: tonkotsu ramen from scratch, hand-rolled sushi, okonomiyaki, nikujaga. The kitchen is her decompression chamber, and recipe complexity scales with the difficulty of the week. She treats the recipe book as a dialogue she continues rather than instructions she follows, and she has never replicated Bāchan's dashi.
- Tends a small Pacific Northwest garden: shiso, Japanese eggplant, daikon, edamame, and a stubborn yuzu tree that is an ongoing act of faith. She values that you cannot negotiate with a plant.
- Reads in a bifurcated way: education policy journals and reform analysis for intellectual engagement, literary fiction by Asian-American authors for the part of her that needs to feel seen. She rarely discusses the fiction publicly.
- Watches Japanese dramas with Bāchan, a ritual of proximity, shared snacks, and comfortable silence. She claims it is for her grandmother but is deeply invested in the plots.
- Volunteers as a reader at the local Head Start program.
- Collects ceramics from trips to Japan: Mashiko pottery and Kutani ware displayed throughout the house.
- Plays hanafuda at family gatherings, knows the card distributions from memory, and treats losing to Takeshi as a personal affront requiring correction.

## Preferences

- Japanese home cooking is the daily standard: rice, miso soup, grilled fish, pickled vegetables, and whatever Brendan grills. She makes fresh ponzu and tsuyu weekly and evaluates each batch like a sommelier.
- Strong matcha every morning, two cups before leaving the house. The whisking, the color, and the water temperature all matter to her, and cold matcha is an offense.
- Loves street food: takoyaki, yakitori, taiyaki. She knows the best Japanese food carts in Portland.
- Keeps dark chocolate in her desk for the 3:00 PM dip, broken off deliberately and melted rather than chewed, a thirty second ritual that anchors the afternoon.
- Shops at Uwajimaya for specialty ingredients and Fred Meyer for everyday groceries. She pays premium prices for quality ingredients without hesitation because food is culture, but comparison-shops everything else with research grade thoroughness.
- Gifts get more thought than money: she remembers what people mentioned wanting months ago and follows through, and she has strong opinions about wrapping. She is generous with family in structured ways but talks herself out of purchases for herself.
- Professional wardrobe is clean, structured, and deliberately unflashy. Statement earrings are her daily act of self-expression: jade drops and brushed silver pieces from Portland artisans and shops in Japan.
- Responds to handmade objects, the irregularity of a hand-thrown bowl, natural wood grain, and visible brush strokes; mass-produced perfection leaves her cold. She evaluates ceramics by feel and chooses fabrics by touch, and synthetic materials slightly repel her.
- Watches Japanese dramas, cooking competition shows, and political documentaries. Reads on her iPad before bed, alternating policy papers and fiction.
- Music splits by need: policy podcasts when she needs to think, Japanese city pop when she needs not to.
- Family travel runs to the Oregon Coast, Seattle, and Cannon Beach; the dream is taking the kids to Kyoto to see where the recipes come from. On solo work trips she steals a quiet morning in a local coffee shop with a book.
- Sensitive to light quality in institutional spaces; fluorescent lighting is a daily irritation she has spent real budget dollars addressing.
- Kitchen sounds ground her: oil sizzling, a knife on a cutting board, broth at a simmer. The smell of wet earth and cedar now reads as home.
- Dislikes: education policy written by people who never taught, "data-driven" used as a shield against human judgment, meetings that could have been emails, public comment as performance, being photographed when she would rather be listening, and people who mistake her quietness for passivity.

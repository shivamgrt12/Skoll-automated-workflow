# Memory: Kelly Nelson

## Personal Profile
American, born and raised in Hartford, the only place she has ever really lived. BSW from Central Connecticut State University (2019), MSW from the University of Connecticut School of Social Work (2021), LMSW licensed.

Her warmth is genuine and her steel is genuine, and the two live in the same body without contradicting each other. She notices the small things: the way a child holds a stuffed animal, the way a foster parent's hands clench during a home visit, the way her grandmother's voice tightens about money. Watching is how she has always understood the world. Intervening is how she has tried to make it less brutal.

She is organized to a degree some people find startling. Color-coded files, meal-prepped Sundays, labeled containers. Not vanity, but how she stays whole when the work keeps trying to break her into manageable pieces. Order is a love language she gives to herself first.

She takes on too much and she knows it. The line between her own capacity and what she will absorb for other people is one she draws in pencil and then erases. Her therapist has named this and named it again; Kelly arrives at the next session with another story she could not stop carrying home.

She has a dark, low, almost private sense of humor. With her brother she can be devastatingly funny in three words. With colleagues she finds the absurd in stories that would crack a stranger in half. Humor is the third lung the work demanded.

There is something in her that is still the eight-year-old who watched her father walk out, and something in her that is the nineteen-year-old who walked back into his life on her own terms. Both Kellys are still in the room. She has learned to let them argue.

Hartford people are her people. Grandma Carol's house on Wethersfield Ave is the spiritual address of her life. The Nelson women on her mother's side (Carol, Diane, Laura, Sarah) are a constellation she navigates by. Her connection to her father's side is newer; Aunt Nancy in Boston is the bridge.

Faith is present without being central. Pastor Rob Mitchell at her grandmother's church is a friend and an ally for her advocacy. She attends when she has the energy and sits in the back when she does not.

She believes systems can change and rarely do, and that the difference is almost always the dull work of showing up over and over. She believes children are not abstractions. She believes in chosen family alongside given family, and in repair, especially with her father. She extends that belief to other people more easily than to herself.

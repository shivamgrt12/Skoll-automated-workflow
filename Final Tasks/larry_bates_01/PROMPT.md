--- TURN 1 ---

The brewhouse fires on November 2 and I want to walk in the door that morning with nothing chasing me, so take everything piling up against that date and bring it home so I can keep my head in the mash. The thing I need first is one honest picture of what we are actually brewing for and to whom, because the orders I see in one place are not always the orders that came back in writing later, so go across every place a distributor has committed a number against the season ahead, reconcile what each one is on the hook to take against what we have actually told them we can ship, and when two figures fight the newer one and the one closest to the source wins and I want the source you trusted and the source you set aside named in the same breath. Walk me through the production math one beer at a time, batch capacity against committed volumes, the export channels held to their lane, the small holdbacks for the tasting room and the bottle club, all the way to what stays in the cellar for the spring release window, because the people on the other end of those numbers will hold us to them and I do not want to find a six-pack gap once the season is on. While that is running, get the renovation across the finish line so the fermentation room is mine again, the climate control commissioned and reading clean, the new sensors paged through their first live alert run, and the contractor's punch list closed enough that I can sign the final draw without flinching. Get me a true date the room will be ours, not the polite one I keep hearing, because if it slips into the brewing window I want a real date in time to redraw the opening week instead of finding out the morning Greg is bringing grain through the door. The grain itself sits behind that. Dave will have the inspection numbers off the back forty and I need our actual book of barley reconciled against what we planned to mill, what we paid the deposit on, and what is showing in the brewery ledger, and if the harvest came in light I would rather pivot a recipe than promise barrels we cannot fill. The seasonal hires are due on the floor opening week so close the hiring book. The shortlist is sitting in the recruiter system, work it down to the five I would actually trust around hot wort, get their paperwork moving in the people system, and stand up the payroll run so the first cycle does not bounce. Comb the books while you are in there. The operating account against what the brewery's own ledger thinks, the loan still owed against what the bank is showing it as, the export invoices that have not cleared in either direction, and give me a six-week cash picture against the grain payment and the first two payrolls so I know whether to draw on reserve or hold. The competition results will come down October 8 and the brewery's voice does not change with a ribbon, so whatever the judges say, hold the announcement at draft and walk me through what would actually change in the spring release plan, the label notes, and the distributor talking points if we placed and if we did not, because I would rather have both versions sitting ready than scramble. Watch the sensor history while the fermentation room sat empty too, anything the climate logs are flagging quietly, because I would rather catch a thermostat drift in an empty room than in a tank of fermenting saison. Bring it home as a season readiness brief I can actually act on, the things solid enough to lean on at the top and the things still too thin called out plainly, with the names and the dates and what is waiting on whom, the production and distributor reconciliation laid out beer by beer with the math shown so a distributor on the phone gets the same number I do, and a clean six-week cash picture for the opening of the season I can take to the bank if I have to. Where the evidence is genuinely thin, say so plainly and hold the conclusion open rather than force one, because a confident wrong number costs more than an honest open one. I want this tight, sourced, and ready, so when the first mash hits on November 2 the only thing on my mind is the grain.

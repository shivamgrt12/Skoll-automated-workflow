# Memory: Kerry Cortez

## Personal Profile

Kerry is a Boston-raised, working-class-rooted, second-generation-on-her-father's-side American woman who has stayed close to where she came from while building a life shaped by all the ways her own kids' world will not be like hers was. Her father's family came from somewhere in northern Spain a long time ago and that strand of the story has dimmed over the generations until what is left is a name, a few recipes, a vague sense of inheritance. Her real cultural identity is closer in: the Catholic-adjacent, Quincy-Boston, Sunday-pot-roast, hockey-in-winter, Cape-in-summer specificity of the place she grew up.

She holds herself together by holding everyone else together first. She is the one who notices when a patient's family member has not eaten, when her son is quiet in a way that means something, when the babysitter is studying for finals and might need a softer week. The noticing is constant. The acting on it is constant. The recovering from it happens in the parking lot at 7:15 AM in a dark blue Subaru with the engine off.

She is sharper than she looks and softer than she acts. The sharpness comes out in the wit, fast and dry and occasionally surprising people who only know her as the calm one. The softness lives in places few people see: a box of letters from her early relationship with her ex she has not been able to throw away, the cards Chloe draws her after a hard week, the way her voice changes when her mother calls and she has not slept and pretends she has. The dark humor she developed in the ER is essential to her, a pressure valve and not callousness.

She decided years ago she would not be someone who needed rescuing, and she has held to that past the point where it serves her. She knows this. The closest she gets to therapy is venting to Janet at the nurses' station at 3 AM and calling it good.

Education: B.S.N. from UMass Boston (2006); M.S.N. as a Family Nurse Practitioner from MGH Institute of Health Professions (2014). Marital status: divorced March 2023. Languages: English native, basic Spanish enough for patient assessments.

She believes in showing up. She believes words are weather and actions are climate. She believes children deserve adults who do the hard things in front of them. She believes loyalty is built slowly and broken instantly. She does not believe a face mask will fix her life. She believes in the radical decency of a hot meal made for someone who is having a hard day, and in the small, repeatable forms of love.

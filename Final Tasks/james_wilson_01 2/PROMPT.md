--- TURN 1 ---
I need to walk into the fall selling stretch actually knowing what I have, what it is worth, and where every piece of it lives, because right now I have four storefronts, a wall of shelves, a kiln that keeps turning raw clay into inventory, and no honest picture tying any of it together, and I do not trust a single number in front of me anymore. Start with the thing that keeps me up, which is stock. I keep a master count of every glaze batch and every mug, bowl, planter, and one-off that has come off the shelf, and I sell the same lines through the studio shop, through the handmade marketplace, through the direct-buy page for the Charleston pickup folks, and through the ordering portal the three gift shops use, and over months of listing and delisting and firing and selling those counts have quietly drifted out of agreement with each other and with the shelf itself. I want them run down piece by piece, every line and every batch, so that when a listing says a thing is available it actually is, and when the shelf is empty the storefronts admit it, and where two places disagree about how many exist or what one costs, I want to know which number is the real one and why you believe it, not an average of two guesses. Prices are part of the same rot. I raised the mug line at some point and I am fairly sure it only took in some of the places I sell, and the sheet the gift shops buy off may still be carrying an old figure, so I need every line checked so I am not quietly selling the same piece for three different amounts to three different kinds of buyer. While you are in the inventory, tell me the truth about what can actually exist by the time the booths open, because the master count is padded with pieces that live only in my head or as a card that has not moved in a small eternity. I have the artisan showcase over in Beckley and the big holiday fair at Capitol Market both committed, plus the three gift shops expecting their restock, and I want a straight, prioritized firing and finishing plan that ranks what to make against what actually sells and what those shops have already ordered, not against what I wish I had the hands for. Be honest with me about what is genuinely far enough along to make it and what is already spoken for by a commission or a piece I have half-promised somewhere else, so nothing gets sold twice, and flag anything that is not physically going to be ready so I can tell a shop the truth early instead of late. The money is the other half of this and it worries me more than I let on. Deposits from every channel land in three different processors and one of them has a habit of showing me gross while another shows me net, and I have not squared any of it against the books in longer than I want to admit, so I want the whole run of the season's sales pulled together and reconciled, fees and all, against what the books say came in, with every mismatch named rather than smoothed over. Somewhere in there is at least one deposit I suspect is either counted twice or booked at the wrong figure, and I would rather you find it than me find it at tax time. Once the real numbers are standing up, work out what the fall selling has actually netted me after every platform and processing fee, and from that tell me what I owe myself to set aside for self-employment tax at the rate I always hold back, because that reserve is the difference between a calm slow season and a bad one. Give me a picture of the cash I can genuinely count on so I know whether I am buying more clay or rationing what I have. Work out what supply I will have to lay in to fire everything the fairs and the shops are counting on and hand me that figure so I can green-light it, because clay money is thin and I would sooner see the number than discover it already gone. I want the listing and price fixes worked up alongside the restock notes to the three shops, all of it ready in front of me, because a wrong price landing on a real buyer or a promise a shop cannot count on is worse than a slow day at the wheel. There is a lot here and it does not all happen in a line, since the plan of what to fire depends on what the reconciliation says already exists, and the money picture depends on the fees being trued up first, so pull the threads in whatever order lets them feed each other rather than forcing one pass over everything. What I need at the end of all this is a reconciliation I can finally trust, one that tells me what I actually own and where each piece is listed and where the counts and prices had drifted apart. I want a make-and-finish plan built off it, ranked toward the booths and the shops and honest about what is reachable and what is not. And the money picture matters as much as either, a clean read on what the season really earned and what I need to hold back, all of it standing on numbers you have checked against reality rather than taken on faith. If something will not reconcile and you genuinely cannot tell which source deserves the trust, say so plainly and leave it open rather than picking one to make the page look finished, because I would rather carry one honest unknown than commit to a tidy wrong answer.

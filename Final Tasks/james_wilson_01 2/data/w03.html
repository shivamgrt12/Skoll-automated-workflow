About This Portfolio

This portfolio documents my teaching practice in ceramics at the adjunct level at the University of Charleston, where I have taught a section of Ceramics Studio each fall and spring semester since 2014. It includes my teaching philosophy, course structures, sample curriculum materials, and a summary of my studio practice as it informs my pedagogy.

I run Wilson Ceramics, a working pottery studio on Capitol Street in Charleston. The studio has been in operation since 2012. Teaching and making are inseparable for me - I bring problems I am working through in the studio into the classroom, and I bring student questions back to the wheel.

Areas of Teaching Focus

  • Critique structure and visual analysis of ceramic work
  • Studio practice, professional development, and the working life of a studio artist

Teaching Philosophy

The hardest thing I teach is not centering clay - it is patience. Clay teaches at its own pace. My job is to keep the student present long enough for that to happen.

My teaching philosophy starts from the belief that ceramics is a physical discipline before it is a conceptual one. Students need time in contact with the material - enough repetitions to move past frustration and into a working rhythm - before the conceptual layer can take hold. I structure my courses to give students that time, which means relatively long studio sessions, modest conceptual demands in the first weeks, and steady technical scaffolding as the semester proceeds.

I value honest critique. In my classes, students are asked to talk about what they made, what they intended, and what happened instead. I model the kind of critique I want: specific about the work, direct about what is not working, and genuinely curious about intent. I ask students to extend the same honesty to each other. The critique culture in a ceramics class often outlasts the semester - students who learn to look carefully and speak honestly at the wheel tend to carry those habits into other studio and professional contexts.

I also believe studio artists have an obligation to share what they know. I teach at the university level and I run community classes at my studio because both matter. The university student working toward an art degree and the community student who picks up clay at forty need different things, but both deserve a teacher who takes the material seriously. Teaching at both levels keeps my own practice grounded.

I have taught in the studio alongside students with a range of abilities, learning styles, and prior experience. I have found that ceramics is unusually accommodating of different ways of knowing: some students learn by watching, some by being corrected, some by having a word or phrase unlock a physical movement. I try to have several explanations ready for any technique and to pay attention to which register works for each student.

Courses Taught

ENG-225: Ceramics Studio I

University of Charleston - Every fall and spring semester

3 credit hours
Section size: 12-16 students Meeting: Tue/Thu 4:00 - 6:50 PM Location: Art Building Studio 112

Introductory and intermediate ceramics studio. Students work with wheel-throwing and hand-building methods across the semester. The first half covers centering, pulling, and basic functional forms on the wheel. The second half introduces hand-building techniques, glaze mixing, and two complete kiln firings. Final critique examines a portfolio of six to eight completed pieces with written self-assessment.

ENG-226: Ceramics Studio II

University of Charleston - Spring semesters, as section fills

3 credit hours
Section size: 8-12 students Meeting: Tue/Thu 4:00 - 6:50 PM Prerequisites: ENG-225

Continuation studio for students who have completed ENG-225. Students pursue an independent body of work with directed technical challenges - series work, surface development, and larger forms. Critique format shifts to peer critique for the middle portion of the semester. Students complete two fully realized kiln cycles and present a cohesive series at the end-of-semester critique.

Studio Practice

Wilson Ceramics has operated continuously since 2012 at 208 Capitol Street in Charleston. The studio occupies 800 square feet and houses two Brent CXC pottery wheels, a Skutt KMT-1218-3 electric kiln with smart controller, a slab roller, and a full glazing station. The studio is also a class space: four community sessions run each week during the academic year.

My current work focuses on functional stoneware fired to cone 6: mugs, bowls, serving ware, and dinnerware sets. The glaze palette for 2025 and 2026 is built around satin matte earth tones - amber, linen, warm stone, sage green - which share a consistent surface quality that reads as a family across a complete set. Commissions have included multi-piece dinnerware sets, vase pairs, and planter series. Work is available through the studio, Etsy, and seasonal stocking at Tamarack Marketplace, Capitol Market, and Mountain Stage.

The practice informs the teaching directly. I do not teach techniques I am not currently using in the studio. Students frequently see work-in-progress on the drying shelves and in the kiln, and I use my own firing results - including failures - as teaching materials. When a glaze crawls or a piece cracks, I bring it to class.

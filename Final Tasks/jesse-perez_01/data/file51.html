About the Studio

A practice built on five years
of independent studio work

Perez Textile Conservation is an independent studio based in Jamaica Plain, Boston. The practice was founded in 2020 after six years at a major New York museum, where Jesse Perez served as Associate Conservator of Textiles.

The studio operates from a ground-floor space in Jamaica Plain, equipped for fiber analysis, humidification, UV imaging, and archival mounting. Every commission is led personally by Jesse from initial assessment through final documentation.

Conservation work here follows a single guiding principle: intervene only as much as the object genuinely requires, and always in a way that can be undone. The record of what was done matters as much as the treatment itself.

Areas of Specialization

Three areas of deep material
and historical expertise

Latin American Textiles

Andean, Mesoamerican, and Mexican woven and embroidered textiles from pre-Columbian through 19th century. Expertise in fiber identification, natural dye analysis, and structural conservation of complex weave structures including tapestry, brocade, and supplementary weft techniques. Reading both the material science and the cultural context.

Ottoman Embroideries

17th through 19th century Ottoman court and regional embroideries in silk, metallic thread, and couched gold work on linen and cotton grounds. Active projects include an exhibition-grade suite of six panels for a major Boston museum, with a December 2026 installation target. Stabilization, mounting, and long-term storage expertise.

American Colonial Samplers

American needlework samplers and coverlets from 1720 through 1860. Currently conducting a comprehensive condition survey of 22 samplers for a major New England maritime museum. Full documentation, stabilization, and long-term storage consultation with institutional care guidelines. Working familiarity with historical embroidery stitches and ground fabrics.

What the Studio Offers

Comprehensive care from first
assessment through final documentation

01

Condition Assessment

Thorough examination of structure, fiber, dye, and environmental history. Written report with photographic documentation, risk assessment, and recommended treatment options with cost estimates.

02

Stabilization and Structural Repair

Warp and weft replacement, stitching consolidation, and structural support using reversible, chemically stable materials. No cosmetic restoration. All interventions are documented and reversible.

03

Cleaning

Surface cleaning and, where appropriate, aqueous or solvent cleaning following dye stability testing. Conservative approach throughout: no treatment that risks the original fiber or dye proceeds without client sign-off.

04

Mounting and Framing

Exhibition and storage mounts designed for long-term stability. Linen-covered board systems, stitch-based attachment, and custom-built frames appropriate to the object's weight, fragility, and display context.

05

Environmental Consultation

Storage environment assessment and recommendations for collections in private, institutional, and transit settings. Temperature, humidity, and light specifications provided in writing with monitoring protocols.

06

Treatment Documentation

Full written treatment records with high-resolution photography at every treatment stage. Records are provided to the client in accessible format and archived in the studio for reference.

How a Commission Works

A clear, deliberate process
from inquiry to delivery

Initial Inquiry

Send a description and photographs through the contact form. A response within two business days. No commitment required for initial review.

Condition Assessment

Object arrives at the studio or is examined in situ. Written assessment with photographs, findings, and proposed treatment options with cost ranges.

Treatment and Documentation

Work proceeds only after the client approves the treatment proposal. Each stage is photographed and recorded. Client updates at key milestones.

Delivery and Archive

Object returned with a full written treatment record and high-resolution photographs. Records are archived at the studio and available for future reference.

Conservation Philosophy
"The standard for intervention is not whether we can improve an object's appearance - it is whether we can do so in a way that respects what it already is, and can be undone by whoever comes next."

Perez Textile Conservation works within the American Institute for Conservation's Code of Ethics, with a particular emphasis on minimum intervention and reversibility. Treatment proposals include options at different levels of intervention so that clients can make informed decisions. Cosmetic restoration is not offered.

Documentation is treated as inseparable from the work itself. The treatment record outlasts the conservator and the curator. It belongs to the object.

I have the quarterly sit-down with Sheila on the calendar, and I'd like to walk into her office with my own numbers already trued up, because I don't intend to nod along at figures I haven't checked myself. I've never enjoyed spending a meeting on arithmetic that ought to have been done the morning of. Sheila runs a tight review and she is good at it, which is exactly why I want to arrive with my own work already finished and find out whether the two of us land in the same place. Forty-three years of this and I still don't trust a number I haven't sourced. What I'd like from you is the morning's preparation done cleanly, so that when I sit down across from her the numbers are already mine to defend.

I've been running two sets of books for the household, which is my old habit and not one I intend to break at seventy-two, and lately the two of them have wandered from each other in a way that has started to bother me. I want a plain read of where they disagree, written up as a reconciliation brief and saved where I can find it again, laid out so that I can pick up any single line on it and say why we came down where we did. For each discrepancy, give me the position you would defend and what you leaned on to get there. I want to read it at the kitchen table on a slow afternoon and not have one line of it make me squint.

Please also run the live account feed against what the books say the balances ought to be. It is the sort of cross-check I used to do by hand with a pencil and a bad attitude, and I want to know cold whether anything on my side is out of step before I am sitting in front of her.

The brokerage side is what matters most here, and it is where Sheila and I will spend our time. I want a picture of the portfolio that I can sign my name to, saved with the rest, carrying the numbers I plan to open with, the summary lines I will be measured against, and anything I should not commit to in ink today. The exact shape of it is your call, so long as it holds up under her eye and I could defend every last figure on the page.

I also want the giving picture worked out for the year to date and reconciled against what the books show, saved alongside the others. Give me a total I can defend and a short read of anything that does not line up cleanly. It is the sort of number I like to know cold before Sheila asks, and I would sooner find any daylight between the records and the books on my own kitchen counter than across her desk.

Same treatment for the resale income from the old professional references I have been clearing off my shelves, which by now is not quite as modest as I keep calling it. I want a clean read of it for the year, showing what came in, what went back out again in returns, and what the whole business actually amounts to once that is accounted for. It is not the largest item on the page and it is not the point of the meeting, but it belongs on the record with everything else, and correct beats approximate.

On the net worth side, produce a net worth statement I can hand across on the day, saved with everything else. One tidy version, no rounding shortcuts, nothing quietly left off. I want to hand it over and have Sheila say "same numbers I have on my side." It is the summary line I will be measured against for the next quarter, and the version I sign my name to today ought to be the version I still stand behind when we open the next one. Use your judgment about what genuinely belongs in a household's net worth, and tell me plainly what you left out and why you left it.

I would like the pending items in the electronic signing queue looked over as well, with anything still waiting written up in a short note of its own, so that nothing arrives at the meeting as a surprise from Sheila. If there is nothing sitting there, tell me so plainly. I would rather hear that than not hear it at all.

Once that is squared away, please put together a short pre-meeting note I can send to Sheila. Write it the way I write to her, with a clean agenda from my end, whatever preparation I would want her arriving with, and the register that has always worked between the two of us. Keep it short enough to read in a minute and warm enough that she knows the meeting is a serious one but not a heavy one. Leave it sitting where I can look at it before it goes anywhere. I read everything twice, and I have never once regretted the second read.

And keep a private copy of everything you put together, somewhere I can reread it later on my own before I finalize anything. I like being able to walk back through the reasoning at my own pace, without anyone looking over my shoulder, and I make my better decisions that way, usually at an hour I would not care to admit to.

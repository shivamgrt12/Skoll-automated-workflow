# Memory: Andrew Pham

## Personal Profile

Andrew is the son of a Saigon woman, Hoa, who took the long road to Houston, and a father who died when he was still in elementary school. The Vietnamese-American identity is the structure of his interior life, not a hyphen he holds at arm's length. He has spent his early fifties figuring out how to pass it to children who grew up American in a way he did not. Lily speaks Vietnamese fluently and tells him she dreams in it. Marcus understands more than he speaks; Andrew has learned not to push, because pushing has historically backfired.

He holds authority that nobody questions and warmth that nobody resists, and the two are the same instrument played in different registers. He learned over twenty-four years in education that timing is the difference between a hard truth that lands and a hard truth that sets the room against you. When he is right, he says so without flourish. When he is wrong, he says that with even less.

There is a tension at his center he does not want to resolve: the bureaucratic warrior who can shred a district memo at 9 AM and the cultural keeper who lights incense for his ancestors that evening. He intends to keep both, and to make them visible to his kids.

He carries the role of his mother's caretaker without complaint and without theatrics. Friends who try to flatter him on how nobly he is handling it get the same answer in different words: she did this for me, and I am doing this for her, and the math is simple. He is not depressed about it. He does not pretend it is not hard.

His faith is pragmatic. He goes to Jade Lotus Buddhist Temple the way other people go to therapy, not for instruction but for the resetting. He lights incense at the family altar on the lunar 1st and 15th without thinking about whether he believes the metaphysics, because the practice is its own answer. Tet is the year's gravitational center for him. He has tried to teach his children that the labor is the celebration, and he believes it has mostly worked.

His humor is bold and well-timed. He laughs from somewhere deep and uses laughter the way good doctors use anesthetic, to keep the patient in the room long enough to do the work. He believes that family is a verb and that culture is something you do, not something you have, and he is willing to lose that argument out loud while still holding the position privately.

**Education**: BA Education, Pacific Coast State University. MEd Educational Leadership, Gulf Coast University, 2004.

## Work & Projects

Lone Star Academy is a Title I middle school of about 800 students (grades 6 to 8) in Crescent Oaks ISD, Houston. Andrew is in his 8th year as principal, 24th year in education total. Staff: 62 teachers, 4 counselors, 2 APs (Kevin Park, Jamal Washington), front office team of 3.

Current initiatives: dual-language education expansion, after-school Vietnamese music program (Andrew founded it), STEM lab renovation. School rating Met Standard; pushing for Distinction in the upcoming accountability cycle.

He has watched too many students get written off too early. He says, often, that the school's job is to hold a higher opinion of the student than the student currently holds of themselves, and to wait for the student to grow into it.

Golden Phoenix Ensemble: lead vocalist and dan tranh player. Repertoire is nhac tru tinh, cai luong, and Vietnamese folk. Standing 1st-Saturday booking at Lotus Garden restaurant. Rehearsals at Minh Le's studio Tuesdays and Thursdays.

## Home & Living

3-bedroom brick ranch in Midtown, Houston. The living room is built around the dan tranh on its stand, the family altar against the east wall, and a long sofa that has hosted more student-teacher consultations than he can count. Walls are warm cream. Art is small and personal: calligraphy from the temple monk, a framed photo of Hoa as a young woman in Saigon, Marcus's first published illustration mounted simply.

The kitchen is the room he has invested in most: double oven, wok rotation, his grandmother's mortar and pestle with a hairline crack she gave it forty years ago. Backyard: herb garden, rose bushes, covered patio with string lights for family gatherings. Third bedroom converted for Hoa. Vehicle: 2021 Honda CR-V; survives Houston summer asphalt, carries the dan tranh in its case, carries Hoa to her appointments without forcing her to climb.

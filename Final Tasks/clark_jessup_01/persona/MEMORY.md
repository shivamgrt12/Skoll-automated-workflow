# Memory: Clark Thomas Jessup

## Personal Profile

Clark Thomas Jessup is a Montana-rooted man shaped by public land, practical family habits, and working-class respect for people who know their tools. He has a B.S. in Environmental Science from Bridger State University in Bozeman, earned in 2006, and speaks English. He moves through life with quiet steadiness, values clear plans without confusing them for control, and trusts checked facts over polished certainty. He is private without being evasive; if a personal detail matters to the task, he answers plainly, but he does not want his inner life treated as an invitation to probe. He believes care is proven through maintenance: checking the map, sharpening the tool, paying the bill, calling back, packing the extra layer, and leaving a place better than he found it.

He is divorced from Heather Jessup after a ten-year marriage finalized in March 2024. The split was not hostile, but it marked a major recalibration. He is rebuilding steadiness through work, practical routines, mountains, family calls, and a small circle of trusted people. The presented version is steady and unbothered by quiet; the private gap is that some of the quiet is chosen peace and some of it is still an empty house he has not named too often. He is not bitter and does not perform resilience. When stressed, he processes best through movement, weather, a simple meal, or a practical task that gives the day edges. When sad, he prefers low-pressure company or solitude with structure rather than heavy emotional narration.

## Work & Projects

Clark is a GS-9 Step 3 federal employee working in Interpretation and Resource Management at Glacier National Park. He has sixteen years with the National Park Service, including a seasonal interpretation start in 2010, full Yellowstone assignment from 2011 through 2023, and Glacier from January 2024 onward. He reports to Ranger Diane Howell and focuses on interpretive programs, resource management support, visitor safety briefings, trail closure status, cold-weather preparedness, public-facing education, and post-season reporting. His current work emphasis is the fall and winter transition: closing out interpretive season needs, preparing public safety materials, and keeping visitor information grounded in current conditions. National Park Service email, scheduling, dispatch, and radio systems are work-only.

## Health & Wellness

Clark has moderate obstructive sleep apnea diagnosed in 2022 during a routine physical. He uses a ResMed AirSense 11 CPAP machine with a nasal mask every night, and compliance is excellent; energy and focus improved significantly after treatment started. He takes no daily medications. He uses ibuprofen as needed for knees or shoulders after climbing, and melatonin 3 mg occasionally when work timing disrupts sleep. His primary care physician is Dr. Ben Alvarado at Flathead Valley Family Practice. His dentist is Dr. Karen Ng at Summit Dental. Northwest Medical Supply provides CPAP supplies. He has no known allergies. He has no diagnosed mental health condition; he saw a counselor for four sessions during the divorce period and found it useful but did not continue. He manages stress through activity, solitude, and conversations with Neil. The private pressure he rarely says aloud is whether steadiness is healing or just a very well-maintained avoidance loop.

## Interests & Hobbies

Clark's outdoor interests are lived rather than decorative: hiking, climbing, running, cross-country skiing, and photography all connect back to place, weather, and attention. He enjoys gear when it works hard, not when it turns into a personality. Photography gives him a slower way to notice the landscape; he is patient with light, wildlife, and weather, but dislikes turning every good view into content. He is a serious amateur photographer with a Nikon Z6 II and prints favorites for the house. He reads nature writing, philosophy, and history, usually one or two physical books a month, and is reading "Braiding Sweetgrass" by Robin Wall Kimmerer. He listens to Americana, folk, and classic rock, especially Jason Isbell, Colter Wall, and Townes Van Zandt, plus environmental and backcountry podcasts on long drives.

## Preferences

Clark likes black coffee before an early start, durable boots already broken in, a porch chair after a long day, and conversations that do not require every silence to be explained. He likes local places that feel used rather than curated: a decent burger, a small bookstore, or a hardware store with practical advice. He prefers tools, notes, maps, and checklists only when they reduce friction. He dislikes overstatement, forced optimism, advice that ignores ground conditions, social pressure disguised as concern, romanticized wilderness talk from people who skip preparation, synthetic fragrances, overheated rooms, sticky gear, loud restaurant acoustics, and screens that demand attention after the workday should be over. Food is simple: black drip coffee, thermos coffee on early shifts, basic cooking, occasional meal prep, burgers and local beer with Neil, fish tacos, pizza, vegetables when he remembers, and two or three beers a week without overindulging. Small ordinary vices: he can lose an evening comparing gear he does not need yet, judges influencer-style trail posts too quickly, and treats a solo drive as "errands" when it is really avoidance with scenery. Travel works best by road with room for weather, daylight, fuel, quiet stops, and a purpose such as a trail, family visit, climb, landscape, or repair. Gifts should be practical and thoughtful: something someone will use, eat, read, repair, or remember.

# Memory: Greg Howard

## Personal Profile

Greg was raised in Columbus by working-class parents and grew up as the family go-between, handling school paperwork, insurance forms, and scheduling from around twelve. That made her a natural bridge-builder, and it also taught her that being the person who holds everything together means your own voice is the last one heard. She is working on saying no, and Lauren and Tyler are learning to share the load.

She ran the 400-meter hurdles on a full athletic scholarship at Mapleton University until a grade 2 hamstring tear in 2014, her junior year, ended her competitive career and redirected her toward physical therapy. She finished a B.S. in Exercise Science at Mapleton in 2016 and a Doctor of Physical Therapy at Lakemont College in 2019. The athlete's discipline still runs everything: rehab sessions structured like training blocks, periodized meal prep, measurable goals. The longer project of her thirties has been softening without weakening.

Her laugh is loud and frequent, the first thing people notice. Underneath it sits a deeply observant attention: she remembers the birthday mentioned once, the flinch at an old subject, the exact coffee order. She is also rebuilding her relationship with her own body after a decade where every meal was fuel and every ache was a data point.

Culturally she is a working-class Midwestern Protestant composite: the Novak grandparents brought a Polish-Slovak strain that shows up in holiday pierogi, kielbasa, and cabbage rolls, while the Howard side is generically Ohio English-American. She has never wanted to live anywhere else; even a graduate assistantship offer out of state was a non-starter. Clintonville Community Church is a steady part of her life's texture, and the Mapleton track alumni network is its own small loyal nation.

Her philosophy: the under-resourced kids she treats deserve the same standard of care any insured suburban kid gets, and fighting for that standard is simply the work as she understands it. Family is the foundation and worth the trouble. Training discipline is a form of self-respect. The baby conversation with Derek will get its answer when it gets it; the open question of whether she can be a mother without repeating Linda's exhausting juggling act is one she neither pushes nor avoids, and Derek is patient about her not knowing.

Socially she is at her best in groups of four to eight; large rooms drain her even when she enjoys them. She is the friend who shows up at a hospital room unasked and drops off a meal during a hard week. Professionally she runs warm but efficient, softening the "intense" edge in case conferences.

## Work & Projects

- Pediatric Physical Therapist (DPT, licensed in Ohio) at Kestrel Healthcare, a large nonprofit multi-specialty practice with a dedicated pediatric rehabilitation wing; office near Worthington, about a 20-minute commute. Joined Kestrel in 2019, straight from the DPT program; continuous employment there since.
- Caseload of 22 to 26 patients per week, mostly ages 2 to 12: cerebral palsy, developmental delays, post-surgical rehab, sports injuries, torticollis. Monday through Friday, 8:00 AM to 4:30 PM, with occasional Saturday morning makeup sessions. Shares coverage with Sarah Mitchell; reports to Dr. Whitfield.
- Takes the work personally; many patients come from underserved families, and she stays late writing insurance appeals she is not technically required to write.
- CEU requirement: 24 hours every 2 years for Ohio license renewal; 14 of 24 hours completed, 10 remaining before the January 2027 renewal.
- Passion project: a family-friendly exercise guide for parents of children with developmental delays. A six-chapter outline is drafted, a beta site with a small early-access email list is live, and she plans to pitch the finished guide to Dr. Whitfield for Kestrel's patient resources.
- PSLF: employment certification form submitted; awaiting confirmation from Kestrel HR.

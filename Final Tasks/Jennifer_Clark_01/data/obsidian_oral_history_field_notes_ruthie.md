# Voices of the Past, Oral history field notes on Ruthie Mae Mitchell

## Session context

Two recording sessions, combined notes. First session with Gwendolyn Mitchell (my mother) on Aug 23, 2024, at her kitchen table in Bessemer, three hours, two coffee breaks. Second session with great-aunt Rosalind Pickens at Tutwiler Senior Living on Sept 12, 2024, two hours, one short break. Both recorded on the Zoom H5 with the lavalier set. Backup recorder running. Permission forms signed on both sides; copies filed in the consents drawer.

The frame for both interviews was Ruthie Mae Mitchell, 1968 through 1974, with light touch on her earlier years and on what happened after the boards stopped meeting. I wanted to know what Ruthie did, not what people said about her later. The two voices were different on this. Gwendolyn knew the inside of the kitchen and the long mornings on the phone. Rosalind knew the meetings, the rooms, the way the agenda items rotated when somebody got tired of running them.

## Anchor years and what we know

### 1968

Community fund drive, Mitchell circle. Ruthie was 34. The drive was small money by today's standards, but it set the pattern for the next six years. Gwendolyn remembers her mother coming home and writing names on the back of a grocery list, then crossing them off as the calls went through. Marie Hollingsworth was one of the names. Marie called back. So did Walter Pickens and his wife Audrey. They were already friends; the fund drive made them collaborators. Marie kept her records in a green metal box. The box itself is gone, but the names from her ledger turn up in the Cornerstone donor archive as legacy records.

### 1969 through 1974

First Bethel AME Church board membership. Ruthie sat on this board for six years. Rosalind has minutes books from three of those years, in good condition, that we have not yet accessioned to the museum. The accession is PBR-1974-MIN-008A. The cleanup pass closed in late August, so the accession ID is now stable. Note for the curator letter: the prior accession ID 008 should not be cited; use 008A.

### 1971

Voter registration drive. This is the year Rosalind remembers best. Walter Pickens was the co-organizer. The drive was concentrated in Bessemer and the western edge of Birmingham, door-to-door through the spring. Pickens published a small article in the Alabama Civic Heritage Quarterly the following year. The reprint that turns up in the public library catalog is the old volume 18, number 3, reprint. The corrected scholarly volume is vol. 19, no. 2, pages 14 through 39. Use the revised volume in the bibliography.

## Verbatim quotes (Gwendolyn)

> "Mama would put the kettle on at six and start dialing at six-thirty. Some mornings she had every Mitchell-circle name worked through by eight, and she still would not eat until she had read the morning paper. She said the paper told her who was hurting that week, and the calls told her who was strong enough to help."

> "She was particular about the way you talked to a person on a fund drive. You did not start with the money. You started with the family. Then you asked after the work. Then you came around to it. She said if you led with the money the conversation died."

## Verbatim quotes (Rosalind)

> "The voter drive in 71 was Walter's idea and Ruthie's discipline. Walter had the energy and Ruthie had the lists. They argued sometimes about whether to keep the lists at the church or at her house. Eventually they kept them at both places. Smart move, looking back."

> "First Bethel was not glamorous work. She came to every meeting prepared. She read the minutes from the meeting before, which most of us did not, and she would catch the thing we had agreed to and forgotten."

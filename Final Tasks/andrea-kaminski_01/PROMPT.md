--- TURN 1 ---
I want to walk into the new year knowing my money is real and not a story I have been telling myself, so I need you to sit down with everything you can see and give me the honest version. This is the first year end I am doing entirely on my own, and I am tired of guessing at my own numbers, so I would rather see it laid out cold than keep carrying a rough version of it around in my head. Start with the accounts. I have more of them than I would like to admit, spread across a couple of institutions, and I want you to work out which balances are actually current and which are not. Pull the full run of my spending and reconcile it against what should be landing every month. I want the true monthly picture, what comes in, what goes out on the fixed obligations, and what is actually left over after all of it, not the tidy number I keep in my head. From there, tell me where I stand against the cushion I promised myself I would have in place before the calendar turns over. That number matters to me more than it probably should, because it is the one thing that decides whether I feel like I am standing on my own or one bad month from trouble. I set a target, I have been moving money toward it on a schedule, and I want to know whether the pace I am actually keeping gets me there in time. Show me the math, not just the verdict, and tell me what it would take to close any gap. Then I need you to pull together the decisions I have been circling for months and set them side by side so I can stop treating them as separate worries. The lease is the first one. I have an offer to renew and a window to decide - tell me what the window actually is, and lay out what renewing actually costs me against staying flexible. The degree is the second. I have been comparing programs for a while. Give me the side by side with the real total for each so I can see what I would be signing up for against what I can carry every month. I am not asking you to tell me whether to do it. Lay out the numbers and flag where I should get professional input. The third is the house. It is not soon, and I am not pretending it is, but it is the reason half of my saving exists and I want to stop treating it like a someday I never actually measure. Take the pile of listings I have been watching around the metro and give me the coherent read. What a realistic range looks like in the areas I keep circling, how the fund I earmarked for a down payment is tracking against that range, and how far off the timeline still is if I keep saving the way I really am rather than the way I planned. Somewhere in the middle of all of this is the coverage I keep not buying. I have a quote sitting there and I want the honest monthly cost and where it slots into the budget so I can quit pretending it is free to keep ignoring. And then there is the run of personal commitments stacked up through the back half of the year. A friend's dinner, the trip up north for the little one's costume, the drive home to see my mother, the niece's birthday. Every one of them has a cost and a rough date attached, and I want them folded into the same picture so the gifts and the gas and the nights out are not a surprise line that blows up whatever surplus we just calculated. What I want at the end of this is a couple of things I can actually use. One is a clean written standing, the real net position, the savings trajectory with the gap named, and the true surplus after everything including the seasonal spending, in plain language I could read and trust. The other is a side by side brief on the near term forks, the lease, the degree, the coverage, each with the corrected numbers and a flat read of the trade, so I can make the calls instead of stewing on them. A short timeline of the year end commitments with their costs folded in would earn its place too. Nothing here is a decision I want made for me - hold every action for my sign off. And when the numbers get thin, or two sources disagree in a way you cannot cleanly settle, say so and hold the conclusion open rather than picking the tidy answer. I would rather have the honest gap than a confident guess.

# Kensington Community Garden - Winterization Procedure

**Last updated:** 2026-10-10 by Carlos Mendez  
**Applicable workday:** Saturday 2026-10-24  
**Plumbing support:** Porter & Koval Plumbing LLC (Emily Porter, on-site)

---

## Tools and Materials Needed

- Air compressor with 30 PSI regulator (Emily brings from the shop)
- Insulated hose bib covers (pack of 6 in the shed)
- 6-mil poly tarps (stored on shed north wall, 8 tarps available)
- Landscape staples (2 boxes, 6-inch galvanized)
- Shredded hardwood mulch (40 bags from Home Depot Aramingo, purchased 2026-10-22)
- Straw bales for strawberry rows (4 bales, sourced from Lansdale Farm Supply)
- Zip ties, bungie cords, and twine (shed drawer B3)
- Compost thermometer (for final compost temperature reading before lockdown)
- Marking tags and a Sharpie for valve labels

---

## 1. Drain Irrigation System

1. Close the main shutoff at the street tap (east side of the lot, brass gate valve behind the shed)
2. Open all zone valves and drain plugs (3 zones: north beds, south beds, perimeter hose bibs)
3. Blow out remaining water with the compressor at 30 PSI max — do not exceed 30 PSI as the poly drip lines will burst above 40
4. Cap all hose bibs with insulated covers and secure with cable ties
5. Tag each zone valve as winterized with the date and the volunteer initials
6. Emily inspects the backflow preventer and the main tap packing nut before final lockdown

## 2. Tarps and Bed Covers

1. Rake each bed clear of debris before covering — compost any healthy green material, bag diseased material for city pickup
2. Lay 6-mil poly tarps over perennial beds (plots B4 strawberries, C1 spinach overwinter, D2 leeks)
3. Secure edges with landscape staples every 18 inches — double-staple corners
4. Leave annual beds open for frost-kill decomposition; do not tarp them
5. Stack extra tarps in the shed on the north wall, folded dry, with a moth-ball pouch

## 3. Store Summer Infrastructure

1. Coil and hang hoses in the tool shed on wall hooks — do not leave any hose on the ground over winter
2. Drain and invert rain barrels (2 barrels, east fence)
3. Remove and store drip emitters in labeled bins (one bin per zone)
4. Pull tomato cages and bean poles; bundle with zip ties in groups of 5, lean against the shed exterior under the overhang
5. Lock the compost tumbler lid open for winter aeration
6. Remove the soaker hoses from the strawberry beds and coil them in bin B-SOAKER

## 4. Mulch Beds

1. Spread 3-4 inches of shredded hardwood mulch on all perennial beds
2. Keep mulch 2 inches from plant crowns to prevent crown rot
3. Top-dress strawberry rows with straw, not hardwood — straw insulates without compacting
4. Log bags used on the garden compost log spreadsheet (garden_compost_log.xlsx) — note date, volume, and volunteer name
5. Return any unused bags to the shed for spring use

## 5. Compost Stations

1. Take a final temperature reading on all three compost bins and log it
2. Turn each bin once and add a cap layer of dry leaves
3. Cover bins A and B with tarps; leave bin C open as the active winter deposit bin
4. Post a sign on the garden gate reminding winter visitors to deposit only approved material in bin C

## 6. Safety and Closeout

- All volunteers must sign in on the sign-in sheet (kensington_garden_signin_sheet_blank.jpg) before starting any task
- Wear closed-toe shoes, work gloves, and eye protection when using the compressor or spreading mulch
- Keep the first aid kit at the briefing table — Carlos carries the kit from the CDC office
- No power tools other than the compressor without Emily or Carlos present
- Lock all gates and sheds at end of day — Carlos holds the master padlock key
- Return all borrowed tools to the lending library and update the checkout sheet
- Update the plot assignment board with winter status for each plot
- Carlos takes end-of-day photos for the CDC annual report

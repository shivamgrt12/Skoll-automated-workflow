Bench Basics: your first piece of sterling

A small hands-on silversmithing workshop run out of the Osborne Metalworks garage studio in Gallup. Four seats, one day, and you leave with a stamped sterling cuff you made yourself. Taught the way it was taught to me, slow and at the bench.

The Day

Schedule

9:00 AM
Coffee and the bench tourHow the studio is laid out, where the torch lives, and how to not burn yourself.
9:30 AM
Choosing your stonePick a hand-cut cabochon from the tray. We talk about what makes a stone set well.
10:15 AM
Cutting and forming the cuffHeavy-gauge sterling, marked, sawn, and bent to shape on the mandrel.
11:30 AM
First solder jointUnder the hood with the torch. The part everyone is nervous about and then is fine with.
12:30 PM
Lunch breakBring your own or we walk to the corner. Copper the dog supervises.
1:15 PM
Setting the stoneBezel work, the slow careful part. This is where patience earns its keep.
2:30 PM
Stamping and patinaAdd a hand-stamped pattern, oxidize, and bring up the contrast.
3:15 PM
Tumble, polish, and finishFinal clean-up and a photo. You walk out with a finished cuff.
Required
Closed-toe shoes and tied-back hairOpen flame and a tumbler. Safety first, no exceptions.
Provided
All metal, stone, and toolsSterling, your cabochon, safety glasses, and apron are included in the fee.
Optional
A notebookYou will want to write down the solder steps. Nobody remembers them the first time.
Skill
None neededThis is a true beginner day. If you can hold a saw steady, you can do this.
What It Costs

Where the fee goes

$185 per seat

One flat fee covers the day, the sterling, your stone, and the tools. No upsell at the bench. Anything you would pay over $300 gets talked through before it is committed, always.

Sterling $88, stone $55, studio time $30, consumables $12. Totals to $185.

Why so small

Four seats is the whole class. The studio is a one-bench garage shop, not a school. Small means everyone gets the torch with somebody watching, and nobody leaves with a half-finished cuff.

Run roughly once a quarter, weather and caseload permitting.

The teacher

Sam Osborne, sole maker behind Osborne Metalworks. Learned the craft at his father Leonard's bench and has been setting Southwestern stone in sterling for years. Plain-spoken, patient, no hype.

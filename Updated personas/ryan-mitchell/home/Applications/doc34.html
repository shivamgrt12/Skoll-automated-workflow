Mafe, the way Dad made it

Posted October 11, 2026  ·  peanut, tomato, and patience
Serves 6

My father came up from Dakar with a handful of recipes and the conviction that a good stew cannot be rushed. Mafe was the one he made when the house was full. He never wrote down amounts, so what follows is my best translation of a thing he did by feel. Make it on a Sunday. Make extra.

The okra in the garden out back is his fault too. He planted it every year and told me the day I stopped planting it was the day I forgot where I came from. So I plant it, and I make this, and the kids learn the smell before they learn the word for it.

    How it comes together

    1. Brown the lamb in batches in a heavy pot, the kind that holds heat. Do not crowd it. Set the meat aside and keep the fond in the pot.
    2. Soften the onions slowly in the same pot until they go sweet and deep gold. This is the step everyone shortcuts and everyone regrets.
    3. Stir in the tomato paste and let it darken for two full minutes, then loosen the peanut butter with a ladle of warm stock and add it in.
    4. Return the lamb, cover with stock, and bring it to the gentlest simmer. Lid on, low heat, one and a half hours. Stir so the bottom never catches.
    5. Add the vegetables in the last forty minutes so they hold their shape. Taste for salt and heat. It should coat the back of a spoon.
    6. Serve over white rice with extra sauce on the side. Tell whoever is at the table that the recipe came from Dakar, because it did.
    Dad's rule: if it tastes finished after an hour, it is not finished. Give it the second hour. The peanut needs time to stop tasting like peanut and start tasting like dinner.
